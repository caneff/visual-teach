Bayes' theorem · Lesson 3

The base-rate fallacy, applied

The famous screening paradox: a rare condition plus an excellent test still gives a low chance of being sick. Here's why — and a checklist so it never fools you again.

⏱ ~15 min Needs: Lesson 2 Intermediate Lesson 3 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Explain why a positive on a 99%-accurate test for a rare condition still means you're probably fine.
  • Work the paradox with natural frequencies — no formula panic required.
  • Run a 5-step checklist on any "positive test, should I worry?" moment.

1The setup that fools almost everyone

A condition affects 1 in 1,000 people (0.1%). The test is genuinely good: it correctly flags 99% of people who have it (sensitivity) and correctly clears 99% of people who don't (specificity). You test positive.

Gut says: "99% accurate, so I'm almost certainly sick." Most people — including most doctors when the problem is posed in percentages — land near 99%.[1] Watch what natural frequencies do to that intuition.

2Count it out: 10,000 people

Instead of multiplying probabilities, just walk a crowd of 10,000 through the test. Each square below is one person.

  • 10 of the 10,000 actually have the condition (0.1%). The test flags about 10 of them.
  • 9,990 are healthy. At 99% specificity the test still mislabels 1% of them — about 100 people.
  • So roughly 110 people test positive, but only 10 are truly sick.

Your real chance of being sick, given a positive result, is about \(10 / 110 \approx 9\%\). The false alarms — drawn from a huge healthy majority — simply outnumber the true cases, even though each individual healthy person was very likely to be cleared.

This is the base-rate fallacy. Ignoring the prior (the 0.1% base rate) and judging by the test's accuracy alone is the error. The base rate is rare, so the pool of potential false positives is vast — and that pool sets your real risk. Lesson 1's lesson, made physical: \(P(\text{positive}\mid\text{sick})=99\%\) but \(P(\text{sick}\mid\text{positive})\approx 9\%\).

3Confirm it with the formula and the calculator

Natural frequencies gave us ~9%. Bayes' theorem must agree — the count is the theorem:

P(\text{sick}\mid +) = \frac{0.99 \times 0.001}{0.99 \times 0.001 + 0.01 \times 0.999} \approx 0.090

Set the calculator to this scenario (it's preloaded). Then experiment: raise the base rate and watch the posterior climb; tighten specificity toward 100% and watch the false alarms drain away. The base rate and the false-positive rate are the two levers that matter.

The reassuring corollary. A positive on a rare-condition screen is usually a reason to take a second, confirmatory test — not to despair. Each positive multiplies your odds; it rarely jumps you straight to certainty. That's why doctors retest.

4Your field checklist — "positive test, should I worry?"

This is the takeaway tool. Run these five steps on any positive result — a medical screen, a fraud flag, a spam filter, a security alert. Your progress is saved on this device.

  • Find the base rate first How common is the condition before any test — 1 in 100? 1 in 10,000? This is your prior, and it anchors everything. If you don't know it, that's your first question to ask.
  • Find the false-positive rate How often does the test flag people who are fine? (That's \(100\% - \text{specificity}\).) A "99% accurate" test still has a 1% false-positive rate — and against a rare condition, 1% is a lot of people.
  • Walk 10,000 people through it Count the true positives (base rate × 10,000 × sensitivity) and the false positives (the healthy majority × false-positive rate). Concrete counts beat slippery percentages.
  • Compute the posterior Real chance = true positives ÷ (true positives + false positives). Compare it to your gut's "99%." The gap is the base-rate fallacy you just avoided.
  • Decide like a Bayesian A positive nudges your belief upward — it rarely settles it. For a rare condition, the right next move is usually a confirmatory test, not panic. Update again with that result.

5Recall & interleave — before you peek

These mix all three lessons. Answer aloud, then reveal.

In one sentence: why does a 99%-accurate test give only a ~9% chance of being sick here?
Because the condition is rare, the few true cases are swamped by false positives drawn from the huge healthy majority — so most positives are false alarms. The base rate, not the accuracy, sets the ceiling.
Which two conditionals are being confused, in Lesson 1's language?
\(P(\text{positive}\mid\text{sick})=99\%\) (the accuracy) is being mistaken for \(P(\text{sick}\mid\text{positive})\approx 9\%\) (your actual risk). The bar got flipped.
You take a second independent test and it's also positive. Roughly what happens to your ~9%?
It rises sharply. Your 9% becomes the new prior for the second test; updating again pushes the posterior well above 50%. Confirmatory testing is Bayes' theorem applied twice — which is exactly why it works.
