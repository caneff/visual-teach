The base-rate fallacy, applied

The screening result that scared you, finally defused — by counting real people instead of trusting a percentage.

⏱ ~15 min Needs: Lesson 2 complete Intermediate Lesson 3 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Solve the classic medical-screening paradox with natural frequencies.
  • Explain why a rare condition keeps the posterior low after a positive.
  • Run a reusable checklist on any "positive test — should I worry?" moment.
This is the mission. A positive result on an accurate test, and you walk away knowing whether to actually worry — not fooled, not panicked, just counting.

1The paradox, in words

A condition affects 1 in 100 people. The screening test is genuinely good: it catches 90% of true cases (sensitivity), and it wrongly flags only 9% of healthy people (false-positive rate). Your test comes back positive. Intuition screams "90% — I'm almost certainly sick." Intuition is reading the bar backwards. Let's count.

Why natural frequencies? Percentages hide the crowd. Gigerenzer's research shows that even doctors get this right far more often when the same facts are phrased as counts — "90 out of 100" — instead of conditional probabilities. So we count people, then confirm with the formula.

2Count 10,000 people

Take a concrete crowd of 10,000. Apply each rate as a count:

GroupHow manyTest positive
Have the condition (1%)10090 (90% caught)
Healthy (99%)9,900891 (9% false alarm)
Everyone who tests positive—981

Of the 981 people who test positive, only 90 are actually sick. So after your positive result:

P(\text{sick} \mid \text{positive}) = \frac{90}{981} \approx 9\%

The formula from Lesson 2 agrees, term for term:

P(\text{sick} \mid +) = \frac{0.90 \times 0.01}{0.90 \times 0.01 + 0.09 \times 0.99} = \frac{0.009}{0.0981} \approx 0.092
Roughly 9%, not 90%. A positive on this "good" test still leaves you about 91% likely to be healthy. The 90% you feared is P(positive | sick) — the likelihood. The number that matters is P(sick | positive) — the posterior. Lesson 1's flipped bar, now with a body count.

3Why the base rate wins

The healthy group is so much bigger that even a small 9% slice of it (891 false alarms) buries the 90 true cases. Rarity is doing the work — not the test's quality. Push the base rate up and the posterior climbs; this is the same lever you pulled in Lesson 2.

4The reusable checklist

This is the whole course, interleaved into one routine. Run it on any "positive test — should I worry?" moment. Your progress is saved in this browser, so you can tick it off against a real result later.

  • Write the conditional you actually want It's P(have it | positive) — not P(positive | have it). Name the hypothesis and the evidence so you don't flip the bar. (Lesson 1)
  • Find the base rate (the prior) How common is the condition in someone like you? Rare base rate → the posterior will stay low no matter how good the test sounds.
  • Find both test rates (the likelihoods) The true-positive rate P(+ | have it) and the false-positive rate P(+ | healthy). The false-positive rate is the one people forget — and it decides everything.
  • Count a crowd of 10,000 True positives = base-rate group × true-positive rate. False positives = healthy group × false-positive rate. Concrete counts beat decimals. (Lesson 2)
  • Posterior = true ÷ (true + false) positives Then compare it to the prior. Did the positive move your belief as far as your gut assumed? Usually far less.
  • Decide — and seek a second, independent test A single positive shifts belief upward; it rarely settles the question. An independent confirmatory test multiplies the evidence and sharpens the posterior.

5Make it stick

Answer from memory, then reveal — this is the retrieval that lasts.

A friend panics: "The test is 95% accurate and I'm positive — I must be sick!" What's the one question you ask back?
"How common is the condition?" Without the base rate, 95% is just a likelihood. If the condition is rare, the false positives from the huge healthy group can still outnumber the true cases — and the posterior stays low.
Two tests are equally "90% accurate." One screens for a 1-in-50 condition, the other for a 1-in-5,000 condition. Same positive result — which should worry you more, and why?
The 1-in-50 one. Higher base rate means more true positives relative to false alarms, so the posterior is much higher. The rarer condition keeps the posterior near the floor even after a positive.
