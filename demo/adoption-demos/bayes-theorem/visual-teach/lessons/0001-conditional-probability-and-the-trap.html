Bayes' theorem · Lesson 1

Conditional probability and the trap

What \(P(A \mid B)\) actually means — and why quietly flipping it is the mistake behind nearly every scary test result.

⏱ ~10 min Needs: nothing — start here Beginner Lesson 1 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Read \(P(A \mid B)\) aloud and say exactly which group it restricts you to.
  • Explain why \(P(A \mid B)\) and \(P(B \mid A)\) are usually different numbers.
  • Spot the swap when someone treats a test's accuracy as your chance of being sick.

1Conditioning is just zooming in

Forget formulas for a moment. A conditional probability is what you get when you throw away everyone who doesn't match a condition and recount inside what's left. That's it. The condition is a spotlight; you only score the people standing in it.

Picture a town of 1,000 people. Some carry umbrellas; some are out in the rain. Here are the counts:

It is raining200 people are out in it
…of those, carrying an umbrella180 people
Carrying an umbrella (rain or shine)250 people
…of those, actually in the rain180 people

"The chance someone has an umbrella given it's raining" zooms into the 200 rained-on people and counts umbrellas: \(180/200 = 90\%\). "The chance it's raining given they have an umbrella" zooms into the 250 umbrella-carriers instead: \(180/250 = 72\%\). Same 180 people in the numerator — different group in the denominator. That denominator swap is the entire idea.

The one-line definition. \(P(A \mid B)\) is a fraction whose denominator is "everyone where \(B\) is true," and whose numerator is "…of those, the ones where \(A\) is also true." Change the condition, change the denominator, change the answer.

2Reading the notation

The vertical bar reads "given" — and everything after it is the group you've zoomed into.

P(A \mid B) \;=\; \frac{\text{count of } A \text{ and } B}{\text{count of } B}

Read aloud: "the probability of A, given B" — meaning, among only the B cases, how often A also holds.

Order matters and it is easy to get backwards. \(P(\text{umbrella} \mid \text{rain})\) is about a rainy crowd; \(P(\text{rain} \mid \text{umbrella})\) is about an umbrella-carrying crowd. The thing before the bar is what you're measuring; the thing after the bar is the room you're standing in.

3The trap: a conditional is not symmetric

Here is the misconception this whole course exists to fix. In general,

P(A \mid B) \;\neq\; P(B \mid A)

They are answers to different questions, so there is no reason they'd be equal. A blunt example makes it stick: nearly everyone out in heavy rain gets wet, so \(P(\text{wet} \mid \text{rain}) \approx 1\). But plenty of wet people got that way from a shower, a pool, a spilled drink — so \(P(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})\) is much smaller. Flip the bar and the number can collapse.

What the test tells you \(P(\text{positive} \mid \text{disease})\)

Of people who have the disease, how many test positive. This is the test's accuracy — often 99%.

What you actually want \(P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive})\)

Of people who test positive, how many really have the disease. This is your real risk — and it can be far lower.

This is the base-rate trap in seed form. Hearing "99% accurate" and feeling "99% likely to be sick" silently swaps \(P(\text{positive} \mid \text{disease})\) for \(P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive})\). Lesson 3 shows how far apart those two can be. For now, just catch the swap.

4Recall — before you peek

Say your answer out loud first, then click to check. Retrieving it from memory is what makes it stick — far more than re-reading.

In \(P(A \mid B)\), which letter names the group you zoom into — the denominator?
B — the condition, the part after the bar. You restrict to all the B cases and ask how often A also holds among them.
Why is \(P(\text{rain} \mid \text{umbrella})\) smaller than \(P(\text{umbrella} \mid \text{rain})\) in our town?
Same 180 people on top, but different denominators: 250 umbrella-carriers vs. 200 rained-on people. More people carry umbrellas than are caught in the rain, so dividing by the bigger group gives the smaller fraction.
A test is "99% accurate." Which conditional is that — and which one do you actually care about?
"99% accurate" is \(P(\text{positive} \mid \text{disease})\) — accuracy among the sick. You care about \(P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive})\) — your risk given your positive result. Different denominators, potentially very different numbers.

5Practice — name the conditional

Each statement describes one conditional. Pick the expression that says the same thing. Wrong picks explain themselves; keep going until it's green.

"Of the patients who truly have the disease, 99% get a positive result."

"Among everyone who tested positive, what share actually have the disease?"

✦ What you earned

A conditional probability zooms into the group named after the bar and recounts. Flip the bar and you flip the denominator — so \(P(A \mid B)\) and \(P(B \mid A)\) are different questions with different answers. "Test accuracy" and "my risk given a positive" are exactly that pair.
