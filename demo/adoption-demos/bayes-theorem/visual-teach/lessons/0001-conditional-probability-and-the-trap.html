Bayes' Theorem · Lesson 1

Conditional probability and the trap

Conditioning is just zooming into a sub-group — and the direction you zoom changes the answer.

⏱ ~10 min Needs: nothing — start here Beginner Lesson 1 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Say what \(P(A \mid B)\) means in plain words.
  • Show, with counts, why \(P(A \mid B) \neq P(B \mid A)\).
  • Name the famous confusion that flips them — before it ever fools you.
Why this matters for your goal: the screening result that scared you — "positive on a 99% accurate test" — is a conditional read in the wrong direction. Get the direction straight here, and the rest of the course is arithmetic.

1What the bar really means

The notation \(P(A \mid B)\) reads "the probability of A given B." The bar \(\mid\) is an instruction: throw away every case where B is false, then ask how often A happens in what's left. You are not looking at everyone anymore — you've zoomed into the B sub-group.

Conditioning = changing the denominator. Unconditional probability divides by everyone. A conditional probability divides by only the group you conditioned on. Same world, smaller crowd.

2A crowd you can count

Forget formulas. Picture 1,000 people at a concert. Two things might be true of any person: they're wearing the band's T-shirt, and/or they know every lyric. Here is the actual crowd:

Group How many
Wearing the T-shirt200
Know every lyric100
Both — shirt and lyrics80

Now zoom, one direction at a time, using only the counts above:

Zoom into the T-shirt wearers (200 people). How many know the lyrics? 80. So \(P(\text{lyrics} \mid \text{shirt}) = \tfrac{80}{200} = 40\%\).

Zoom into the lyric-knowers (100 people). How many wear the shirt? 80. So \(P(\text{shirt} \mid \text{lyrics}) = \tfrac{80}{100} = 80\%\).

Same 80 people on top — different crowd on the bottom. 40% one way, 80% the other. The overlap never changed; the group you divided by did. That is the whole reason \(P(A \mid B) \neq P(B \mid A)\).

3When the gap is enormous

The concert numbers are close enough to feel harmless. Real screening is not. Consider: nearly everyone who is pregnant is a woman, so \(P(\text{woman} \mid \text{pregnant}) \approx 100\%\). But most women are not pregnant right now, so \(P(\text{pregnant} \mid \text{woman})\) is small. Flip the bar and you go from "almost certain" to "unlikely" — same two facts.

This is the trap. Confusing \(P(A \mid B)\) with \(P(B \mid A)\) has a name: the confusion of the inverse (in courtrooms, the "prosecutor's fallacy"). It's what makes "the test is positive in 99% of sick people" feel like "you're 99% likely to be sick." Those are opposite directions of the bar.

4Catch it yourself

A clinic reports: "Of patients who truly have the condition, 95% test positive." Which probability is that?

Which statement matches "95% of those who have it test positive"?

5Make it stick

Answer out loud from memory first, then reveal. Retrieval is what builds retention.

Why can \(P(A \mid B)\) and \(P(B \mid A)\) be so different when the overlap is identical?
Because they divide the same overlap by different groups. The bar fixes which crowd you've zoomed into, and that crowd is the denominator. Different denominator → different fraction, even with the same number on top.
In "80% of burglars wear gloves," which direction is the bar — and what would the inverse claim?
That's \(P(\text{gloves} \mid \text{burglar})\). The inverse, \(P(\text{burglar} \mid \text{gloves})\), is tiny — almost everyone wearing gloves is just cold. Treating the first as the second is the prosecutor's fallacy.

✦ What you earned

You can read the bar in the right direction: \(P(A \mid B)\) zooms into B and counts A. Flipping it is a named mistake, not a rounding error — and it's the engine of every scary-test story.
