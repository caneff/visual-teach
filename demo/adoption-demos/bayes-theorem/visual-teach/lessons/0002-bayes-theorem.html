Bayes' theorem

One formula turns the test's accuracy into your actual risk. We'll derive it in a single honest step, then drive it with a live calculator.

⏱ ~15 min Needs: Lesson 1 Beginner Lesson 2 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Write Bayes' theorem and name all four parts: prior, likelihood, evidence, posterior.
  • Derive it in one line from the definition of conditional probability.
  • Read a worded scenario, pull out the prior and likelihood, and compute the posterior.
Why this matters for your goal: Lesson 1 named the swap between \(P(\text{positive} \mid \text{disease})\) and \(P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive})\). Bayes' theorem is the bridge between them — the exact machine for converting "the test is accurate" into "here's how worried I should actually be."

1Start with counts, not symbols

We lead with frequencies — they're how the answer becomes obvious. Suppose 1 in 100 people has a condition, and a test catches 80% of real cases but also wrongly flags about 10% of healthy people. Out of 10,000:

  • 100 have the condition → the test flags about 80 of them (true positives).
  • 9,900 are healthy → the test flags about 950 of them anyway (false positives).
  • So 1,030 people test positive, but only 80 are actually sick.

Your risk given a positive result is \(80 / 1030 \approx 7.8\%\) — not 80%. Bayes' theorem is just this counting argument written compactly so you never have to redraw the population by hand.

2The formula

Write \(H\) for the hypothesis ("I have the condition") and \(E\) for the evidence ("the test was positive"). Then:

P(H \mid E) \;=\; \frac{P(E \mid H)\,\cdot\,P(H)}{P(E)}

Each piece has a name worth memorizing — they recur everywhere:

Prior
\(P(H)\) — how likely the hypothesis was before the test. Here, the base rate: 1%.
Likelihood
\(P(E \mid H)\) — how well the evidence is explained if the hypothesis is true. Here, the test's hit rate on real cases: 80%.
Evidence
\(P(E)\) — how often the evidence shows up at all, sick or not. Here, the total positive rate: ~10.3%.
Posterior
\(P(H \mid E)\) — your updated belief after seeing the evidence. The answer you wanted: ~7.8%.

3Where it comes from — one honest step

It's not an incantation. Start from Lesson 1's definition, written two ways for the same overlap \(P(H \text{ and } E)\):

P(H \mid E)\,P(E) \;=\; P(H \text{ and } E) \;=\; P(E \mid H)\,P(H)

Both outer expressions equal the overlap, so they equal each other. Divide both sides by \(P(E)\) and you have the theorem:

P(H \mid E) \;=\; \frac{P(E \mid H)\,P(H)}{P(E)}
The denominator is just bookkeeping. When you can't read \(P(E)\) off directly, expand it as "true positives plus false positives": \(P(E) = P(E \mid H)P(H) + P(E \mid \text{not }H)\,P(\text{not }H)\). That's the 80 + 950 from the count above — the same arithmetic, no new idea.

4Drive it yourself

This is the skill, not a spectator sport: set the three numbers and watch the posterior move. It's loaded with the example above. Try dropping the base rate to 0.1 (a rarer condition), or pushing specificity to 99.9 (a near-perfect test) — and notice which lever actually rescues the posterior.

Read it back to the formula. The calculator's "real positives" is \(P(E \mid H)P(H)\); "real + false alarms" is \(P(E)\); the headline percentage is the posterior \(P(H \mid E)\). Same theorem, just counted out.

5Recall — before you peek

Answer from memory first; then reveal.

Name the four parts of \(P(H\mid E)=\dfrac{P(E\mid H)P(H)}{P(E)}\).
Posterior \(P(H\mid E)\), likelihood \(P(E\mid H)\), prior \(P(H)\), and evidence \(P(E)\) on the bottom.
In one step, why does \(P(H\mid E)\,P(E)=P(E\mid H)\,P(H)\)?
Both sides equal the same overlap \(P(H \text{ and } E)\) — the definition of conditional probability written from each side. Divide by \(P(E)\) to get the theorem.
"5% of widgets are defective; a scanner flags 90% of defects and 3% of good ones." What's the prior and the likelihood?
Prior \(P(\text{defective}) = 5\%\). Likelihood \(P(\text{flag}\mid\text{defective}) = 90\%\). (The 3% is the false-positive rate — specificity is 97%.) Plug into the calculator above and you'll get a posterior near 61%.

6Practice — find the parts

Same scenario each time, different question. Pick the right value; wrong picks explain themselves.

Scenario: 2% of travelers carry contraband. A scanner alerts on 95% of real smugglers, and also alerts on 8% of innocent travelers.

Which number is the prior, \(P(H)\)?

Which number is the likelihood, \(P(E\mid H)\)?
