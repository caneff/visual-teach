Bayes' theorem

One formula flips the bar correctly. We'll derive it in a single honest step — no magic — then use it to update a belief with evidence.

⏱ ~15 min Needs: Lesson 1 complete Intermediate Lesson 2 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Name the four parts: prior, likelihood, evidence, posterior.
  • Derive Bayes' theorem from the definition of conditional probability.
  • Read a scenario, pick out the prior and likelihood, and compute the posterior.
Why this matters for your goal: this is the exact machine that turns "positive test" into "actual chance you're sick." Build it once here and the screening paradox in Lesson 3 becomes a calculation you trust.

1The formula

P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H)\,\cdot\,P(H)}{P(E)}

\(H\) is a hypothesis ("I have the condition"); \(E\) is the evidence ("the test was positive"). The formula takes the conditional you can easily measure — \(P(E \mid H)\) — and gives you the one you actually want — \(P(H \mid E)\). It flips the bar, the move Lesson 1 warned you never to do by hand.

2Where it comes from (one step)

It is not handed down — it falls straight out of the definition of conditional probability. The overlap \(P(H \cap E)\) can be written two ways, by zooming from either side:

P(H \cap E) = P(H \mid E)\,P(E) \qquad\text{and}\qquad P(H \cap E) = P(E \mid H)\,P(H)

Both equal the same overlap, so set them equal and divide by \(P(E)\):

P(H \mid E)\,P(E) = P(E \mid H)\,P(H) \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H)\,P(H)}{P(E)}
That's the whole trick. The two conditionals share one numerator — the overlap. Bayes is just bookkeeping that says: to flip the bar, rescale by the priors you divided away.

3The four names

Prior — P(H)Belief before evidence — the base rate.
Likelihood — P(E | H)How well the evidence fits the hypothesis.
Evidence — P(E)How common the evidence is overall — true hits plus false alarms.
Posterior — P(H | E)Updated belief after evidence — the answer.
The denominator is where people stall. \(P(E)\) is rarely handed to you directly. You build it: \(P(E) = P(E \mid H)P(H) + P(E \mid \lnot H)P(\lnot H)\) — the real hits plus the false alarms. Count both groups and add.

4Work one end to end

A mailbox filter. 1 in 100 emails is phishing. The filter flags 90% of phishing emails, and falsely flags 5% of legitimate ones. An email just got flagged — how worried should you be that it's really phishing?

First, name the parts before touching arithmetic:

Which number is the prior, P(phishing)?

Now count a crowd of 10,000 emails instead of juggling decimals:

GroupCountFlagged
Phishing (1%)10090  (90% of 100)
Legit (99%)9,900495 (5% of 9,900)
All flagged emails—585

Of the 585 flagged emails, only 90 are truly phishing. So the posterior is \(\tfrac{90}{585} \approx 15\%\). Now confirm it with the formula — the counts and the algebra must agree:

P(\text{phish} \mid \text{flag}) = \frac{0.90 \times 0.01}{0.90 \times 0.01 + 0.05 \times 0.99} = \frac{0.009}{0.0585} \approx 0.154
A 90% filter, and a flagged email is still only ~15% phishing. The likelihood is loud (90%) but the prior is tiny (1%), and 5% of a huge legit pile is a lot of false alarms. The posterior lands far below the likelihood. That gap is the lesson.

5Your turn to compute

Same filter, new mailbox: now 4 in 100 emails are phishing (prior 0.04). The filter still flags 90% of phishing and 5% of legit. Out of 10,000: phishing = 400 → 360 flagged; legit = 9,600 → 480 flagged. An email is flagged — what's the posterior?

What is P(phishing | flagged) here?

6Make it stick

Recall before revealing — produce the answer from memory.

Reconstruct Bayes' theorem from the definition of conditional probability.
The overlap can be split two ways: P(H∩E) = P(H|E)·P(E) and P(H∩E) = P(E|H)·P(H). Setting them equal and dividing by P(E) gives P(H|E) = P(E|H)·P(H) / P(E).
Why is the posterior usually far below the likelihood for a rare hypothesis?
Because the evidence denominator P(E) is dominated by false alarms when the hypothesis is rare. A small prior times a big likelihood is still small next to "false-alarm rate × the huge rest of the world."
