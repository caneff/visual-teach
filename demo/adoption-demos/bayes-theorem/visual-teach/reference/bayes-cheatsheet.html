Bayes' Theorem · Reference

Bayes in one page

The formula, the names, the count-first recipe, and the worry checklist — compressed for quick review and printing.

1The formula

P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H)\,\cdot\,P(H)}{P(E)}

Read it as: new belief = (how well the evidence fits the hypothesis) × (old belief), scaled by how common the evidence is overall. It comes in one step from the definition of conditional probability, \(P(H \mid E) = \tfrac{P(H \cap E)}{P(E)}\).

2The four names

Prior — \(P(H)\) What you believed before the evidence. The base rate.
Likelihood — \(P(E \mid H)\) How probable the evidence is if the hypothesis is true. The test's hit rate.
Evidence — \(P(E)\) How probable the evidence is overall, true cases plus false alarms.
Posterior — \(P(H \mid E)\) What you should believe after the evidence. The answer you want.
The flip that costs you — \(P(E \mid H)\) is not \(P(H \mid E)\). "Positive given sick" (the likelihood, large) is not "sick given positive" (the posterior, often small). Mixing them up is the base-rate fallacy.

3Count-first recipe (natural frequencies)

Skip the percentages. Imagine a concrete crowd and just count.

  1. Pick a round population: 10,000 people.
  2. Apply the base rate → how many actually have it.
  3. Of those, apply the true-positive rate → true positives.
  4. Of the rest (the healthy), apply the false-positive rate → false positives.
  5. Posterior = true positives ÷ (true positives + false positives).

4Worked example — the screening paradox

Base rate 1 in 1,000; test is 99% accurate both ways. Out of 10,000 people:

GroupCountTest positive
Have the condition10≈ 10 (true positives)
Do not have it9,990≈ 100 (false positives)
Posterior after a positive10 ÷ (10 + 100) ≈ 9%
Why so low? The condition is rarer than the test's error rate. A 1% false-alarm rate over 9,990 healthy people (≈100 alarms) swamps the 10 real cases. Rarity is doing the heavy lifting, not the test.

5"Positive test — should I worry?" checklist

  1. What is the base rate of this condition? (Rare → posterior stays low.)
  2. What is the test's false-positive rate? Even 1–5% matters a lot when the condition is rare.
  3. Build the crowd of 10,000 and count true vs. false positives.
  4. Posterior = true ÷ (true + false). Compare it to the prior — did it really move that far?
  5. A positive shifts belief up; it rarely settles the question. Confirm with a second, independent test.
