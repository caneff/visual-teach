Bayes' theorem · Reference

Glossary

The canonical language for this course. Every lesson uses these terms in exactly this sense — when in doubt, this page wins.

1The conditional

Conditional probability
The probability of one event computed within the cases where another holds. Written \(P(A \mid B)\): restrict to all the \(B\) cases, then ask how often \(A\) also holds. Avoid: "the odds of A and B."
\(P(A \mid B)\)
Read "the probability of A given B." The thing before the bar is what you measure; the thing after the bar is the group you've zoomed into (the denominator).
Asymmetry
The fact that \(P(A \mid B) \neq P(B \mid A)\) in general — they have different denominators, so they answer different questions. The single most important fact in this course.

2The four parts of the theorem

P(H \mid E) \;=\; \frac{P(E \mid H)\,\cdot\,P(H)}{P(E)}
Prior
\(P(H)\) — your belief in the hypothesis before the evidence. In screening, the base rate. Avoid: "the starting guess."
Likelihood
\(P(E \mid H)\) — how well the hypothesis predicts the evidence. For a test, its sensitivity. Avoid: using "likelihood" to mean "probability of the hypothesis."
Evidence
\(P(E)\) — how often the evidence occurs overall, across every hypothesis. The denominator; equals true positives + false positives. Avoid: "marginal" (correct but jargon).
Posterior
\(P(H \mid E)\) — your updated belief after the evidence. The number you actually wanted. Avoid: "the final answer" — there's always a next update.

3Screening vocabulary

Base rate
How common the condition is in the population, before any test — the prior for a screening problem. Avoid: "prevalence" loosely; here base rate is the prior.
Sensitivity
\(P(\text{positive} \mid \text{has it})\) — the share of true cases the test catches. The likelihood. Avoid: "accuracy" (which blurs two different rates).
Specificity
\(P(\text{negative} \mid \text{healthy})\) — the share of healthy people the test correctly clears.
False-positive rate
\(1 - \text{specificity}\) — the share of healthy people the test wrongly flags. Against a rare condition, this is what sinks the posterior.
True / false positive
A true positive is a flagged person who has it; a false positive is a flagged person who doesn't. Among all positives, their ratio is the posterior.

4The named mistake & its fix

Base-rate fallacy
Judging \(P(H \mid E)\) from the likelihood alone while ignoring the prior — e.g. reading "99% accurate" as "99% likely sick." Avoid: "base-rate neglect" is a synonym; we use base-rate fallacy.
Natural frequencies
Stating chances as counts out of a concrete population ("10 of 10,000") instead of percentages. Demonstrably makes Bayesian reasoning easier for laypeople and experts alike.†
Posterior odds
The odds form of the theorem: posterior odds = prior odds × likelihood ratio. The shortcut behind "each positive test multiplies your odds."

† Gigerenzer & Hoffrage's natural-frequency result; see McDowell & Jacobs (2015).
