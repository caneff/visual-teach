Cheat sheet

Everything in three lessons, compressed to one page you can keep beside you. Lead with the counts; use the formula to confirm.

1The formula

\overbrace{P(H \mid E)}^{\text{posterior}} \;=\; \frac{\overbrace{P(E \mid H)}^{\text{likelihood}}\,\cdot\,\overbrace{P(H)}^{\text{prior}}}{\underbrace{P(E)}_{\text{evidence}}}

And the expanded denominator, when you must build \(P(E)\) from scratch — "true positives plus false positives":

P(E) = P(E \mid H)\,P(H) + P(E \mid \lnot H)\,P(\lnot H)

2The natural-frequency recipe

Skip the algebra; walk a crowd through the test instead.

Step 1Take 10,000 people (any round number that makes the base rate a whole count).
Step 2Have it: 10,000 × base rate. Healthy: the rest.
Step 3True positives: (have it) × sensitivity.
Step 4False positives: (healthy) × (1 − specificity).
Step 5Posterior = true positives ÷ (true positives + false positives).

3The worked canonical case

Base rate 0.1%, sensitivity 99%, specificity 99%, out of 10,000:

GroupCountTest positive
Have it10≈ 10 (true positives)
Healthy9,990≈ 100 (false positives)
All positives—≈ 110 → posterior ≈ 9%
The one idea. When the condition is rare, false positives from the huge healthy majority outnumber the true cases — so \(P(\text{sick}\mid+)\) stays low even when the test is excellent. The base rate sets the ceiling, not the accuracy.

4"Positive test, should I worry?" — 5 steps

  1. Base rate first. How common is it before any test? (= prior)
  2. False-positive rate. 1 − specificity. A "99% accurate" test still flags 1% of the healthy.
  3. Walk 10,000 people. Count true vs. false positives.
  4. Posterior. True ÷ (true + false). Compare to your gut.
  5. Decide. A positive nudges, rarely settles — confirm with a second test, then update again.

5Traps to dodge

  • Flipping the bar: "99% accurate" is \(P(+\mid\text{sick})\), not \(P(\text{sick}\mid+)\).
  • Dropping the prior: judging by accuracy alone is the base-rate fallacy.
  • Treating one test as final: the posterior becomes the prior for the next piece of evidence.
