Glossary

The canonical language for this course. Every lesson uses these terms — and only these.

Core terms

Conditional probability — P(A∣B), "the probability of A given B." The probability of A computed only among cases where B is true. Conditioning = zooming into the sub-group where B holds. (Lesson 1)
Avoid: "the probability of A and B," "the odds of A."

Confusion of the inverse — the error of treating P(A∣B) and P(B∣A) as the same number. They differ because they zoom into different-sized groups. (Lesson 1)
Avoid: "inverse fallacy," "flipping the conditional" (use as informal gloss only).

Bayes' theorem — the rule that recovers the conditional you want from the one you have: P(H∣E)=P(E∣H)⋅P(H)P(E). (Lesson 2)
Avoid: "Bayes' rule" / "Bayes' law" (synonyms; pick Bayes' theorem here).

The four parts

Prior — P(H). Your belief in the hypothesis before the evidence; the base rate.
Avoid: "initial guess," "assumption."

Likelihood — P(E∣H). If the hypothesis were true, how often you'd see this evidence. For a test, this is its sensitivity.
Avoid: "probability of the hypothesis" (that's the posterior), "accuracy."

Evidence — P(E). How often the evidence occurs at all, across true and false hypotheses: P(E∣H)P(H)+P(E∣¬H)P(¬H). The normaliser.
Avoid: "marginal," "total probability" (correct but jargon — use evidence).

Posterior — P(H∣E). Your updated belief after seeing the evidence. The thing you wanted.
Avoid: "the answer," "the result" (ambiguous — name it the posterior).

Applied terms

Base rate — the prior expressed as a population frequency (e.g. "1% of people"). Same idea as the prior, phrased as how common the hypothesis is at large. (Lesson 3)

Base-rate fallacy — ignoring the base rate and judging from the test's accuracy alone, so a positive result feels far more conclusive than it is. The headline error for rare conditions. (Lesson 3)
Avoid: "base-rate neglect" (synonym; pick base-rate fallacy).

Natural frequencies — statistics as plain counts over one fixed population ("70 of 992 women…") instead of conditional percentages. Easier for humans to reason with; the recommended first move on any screening problem. (Lesson 3)

True / false positive — among everyone who tests positive: those who really have the condition (true positives) versus those who don't but tested positive anyway (false positives). The posterior is true positives ÷ all positives.

Sensitivity — P(positive∣disease), the test's catch rate. Specificity — P(negative∣healthy); 1 − specificity is the false-alarm rate.
