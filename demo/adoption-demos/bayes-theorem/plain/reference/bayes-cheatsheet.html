Bayes’ theorem — cheat sheet & glossary

The compressed essence of the three lessons. Built to print on one page.

The formula

Pposterior (H∣E) = P(E∣H) ⋅ P(H) P(E)

posterior = likelihood × prior ÷ evidence

with   P(E) = P(E|H)·P(H) + P(E|¬H)·P(¬H)

The trap (Lesson 1)

P(A | B) ≠ P(B | A). The quoted “accuracy” of a test is P(positive | sick). What you want is P(sick | positive). Same overlap, different denominator. Conditioning = zoom into the sub-group, then ask your question.

The fix (Lesson 3)

Count natural frequencies out of 10,000 people. Rarity of the condition (the base rate) usually means false positives swamp true positives, so a single positive on an accurate test is often weak news.

The natural-frequency recipe

For any “positive test, should I worry?” problem:

  1. Start with 10,000 people.
  2. Split by the base rate: sick vs. healthy.
  3. Apply the hit rate to the sick → true positives.
  4. Apply the false-positive rate to the healthy → false positives.
  5. Posterior = true positives ÷ (true + false positives).

Glossary

The course adheres to these terms. When in doubt, this is the source of truth.

Conditional probability — P(A | B)
The probability of A restricted to the cases where B holds. Read “A given B.” The bar resets the denominator to “only the B cases.”
Prior — P(H)
Your belief in the hypothesis before the evidence. In screening, the base rate — how common the condition is.
Likelihood — P(E | H)
If the hypothesis were true, how probable is this evidence? The test’s sensitivity / hit rate. Not your answer.
Evidence (marginal) — P(E)
How probable the evidence is across all hypotheses. The normaliser. Equals true positives + false positives, as a fraction.
Posterior — P(H | E)
Your revised belief after folding in the evidence. In screening, the positive predictive value. The thing you actually wanted.
Base rate
The unconditioned frequency of the condition in the population. Synonym for the prior in screening contexts.
Base-rate fallacy
Judging P(H | E) while ignoring the base rate P(H) — typically by mistaking it for the likelihood. Produces wild over-estimates for rare conditions.
False-positive rate
P(positive | healthy). Applied to the (large) healthy group, this is what generates the flood of false alarms.
Sensitivity / hit rate
P(positive | sick). The fraction of true cases the test catches. Equals the likelihood in a screening problem.
Confusion of the inverse
Treating P(A | B) and P(B | A) as equal. The Lesson 1 trap; the prosecutor’s fallacy is the courtroom version.
