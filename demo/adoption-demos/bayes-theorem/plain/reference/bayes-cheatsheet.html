Cheat sheet

The compressed essence. Print it, pin it, glance at it. Designed to be enough on its own.

The theorem

P(H∣E) = P(E∣H) ⋅ P(H) P(E)

posterior = likelihood × prior ÷ evidence. H = hypothesis, E = evidence.

The four parts

P(H)priorbelief before — the base rate
P(E|H)likelihoodtest's hit rate (sensitivity)
P(E)evidencehow common E is, overall (the normaliser)
P(H|E)posteriorupdated belief after — what you want

Build the evidence yourself: P(E)=P(E∣H)P(H)+P(E∣¬H)P(¬H)

The trap (don't flip the bar)

P(A∣B)≠P(B∣A)

"Test is 99% accurate" is P(positive|sick). You want P(sick|positive). Different denominators — the confusion of the inverse.

"Should I worry?" — the 6-step checklist

  1. Name H (have it) and E (tested positive).
  2. Were you handed P(E|H) or P(H|E)? Don't confuse them.
  3. Find the base rate P(H). Small → expect a surprise.
  4. Picture 10,000 people. Count true positives & false positives.
  5. Posterior = true ÷ (true + false) positives.
  6. Confirm with the formula for the exact decimal.

Worked anchor (memorise the shape, not the digits)

1% base rate · 99% sensitive · 5% false alarms · positive result:

True positives100 × 99% = 99
False positives9,900 × 5% = 495
P(disease | positive)99 ⁄ 594 ≈ 17%

Accurate test, positive result, still only ~1 in 6 — because the disease is rare. That's the base-rate fallacy in one table.
