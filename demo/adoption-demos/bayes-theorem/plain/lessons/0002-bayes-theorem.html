Bayes' theorem

The one formula that turns the reverse question into the right number — and where it comes from.

Why this matters for you: Lesson 1 let you spot the flip. This lesson lets you compute the number you actually want — P(H∣E), the chance your belief is true given the evidence. See the mission.

The formula

P(H∣E) = P(E∣H) ⋅ P(H) P(E)

Four named parts. Learn the names — they are how every scenario gets decoded:

SymbolNameIn words
P(H)priorhow much you believed the hypothesis before the evidence — the base rate.
P(E | H)likelihoodif the hypothesis were true, how often you'd see this evidence.
P(E)evidencehow often the evidence shows up at all, true hypothesis or not.
P(H | E)posterioryour updated belief after seeing the evidence. The goal.

Read it as a sentence: your updated belief is your old belief, re-weighted by how much this evidence points at it, divided by how common the evidence is overall. Rare evidence that the hypothesis predicts well moves you a lot; common evidence moves you little.

It isn't magic — derive it in one step

Bayes' theorem is not a new law of nature. It falls straight out of the Lesson 1 definition of a conditional probability. Start with the definition, written two ways for the same overlap P(H∧E)1:

P(H∣E) = P(H∧E)P(E) and P(E∣H) = P(H∧E)P(H)

The second equation rearranges to P(H∧E)=P(E∣H)⋅P(H). Drop that into the top of the first equation and you have Bayes' theorem. The overlap is the bridge: both conditionals are built from the same intersection, so each is reachable from the other.2

Where the denominator comes from

In practice you rarely get P(E) handed to you. You build it by adding up every way the evidence can occur — when H is true and when it isn't:

P(E) = P(E∣H)⋅P(H) + P(E∣¬H)⋅P(¬H)

(¬H means "H is false.") This is the line that the base-rate fallacy forgets — hold onto it for Lesson 3.

Worked example — a spam filter

An email just arrived containing the word "free." Is it spam? Here's what we know:

  • Prior — P(spam)=0.20. One in five emails is spam.
  • Likelihood — P("free"∣spam)=0.90. Most spam shouts "free."
  • And P("free"∣not spam)=0.10. Real mail says "free" sometimes too.

First, identify the parts yourself — this is the skill that survives when the numbers change:

Which quantity is the likelihood in this scenario?

That's the prior — your belief before reading the email. The likelihood points the other way: evidence given hypothesis.
Correct. Likelihood = P(evidence | hypothesis) = P("free" | spam). It asks: if this were spam, how often would I see "free"?
That's the posterior — the goal, not an input. We compute it from the prior and likelihood.

Now work the posterior. Try it on paper before you reveal — build the evidence first, then divide:

Step 1 — the evidence, P("free"). Add the two ways "free" can appear:

P("free")= (0.90⋅0.20) + (0.10⋅0.80) =0.18+0.08=0.26

Step 2 — Bayes' theorem.

P(spam∣"free") = 0.90⋅0.20 0.26 = 0.180.26 ≈0.69

The prior was 20%; the evidence pushed it to a ≈ 69% posterior. The word "free" is strong evidence — but notice it's still not the 90% from the likelihood. You updated; you didn't just copy the test's accuracy across. That gap is the entire point.
