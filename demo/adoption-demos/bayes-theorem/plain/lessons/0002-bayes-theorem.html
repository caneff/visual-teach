Bayes’ theorem

The machine that flips P(E | H) into P(H | E) — and it’s not magic.

In Lesson 1 you saw the trap: the world hands you P(evidence | hypothesis), but you need P(hypothesis | evidence). Bayes’ theorem is the exact exchange rate between those two. This lesson gives you the formula, names its four parts, derives it in a single honest step, and then has you run it on a worded scenario yourself.

The formula

P(H∣E) = P(E∣H) ⋅ P(H) P(E)

Read it aloud as a sentence: “My updated belief in the hypothesis, after seeing the evidence, equals how well the hypothesis predicted that evidence, times how much I believed it beforehand, scaled down by how expected the evidence was overall.”1 Four named parts:

SymbolNamePlain meaning
P(H)priorWhat you believed before the evidence — often the base rate.
P(E | H)likelihoodIf the hypothesis were true, how likely is this evidence? (This is the “test accuracy” number.)
P(E)evidenceHow likely the evidence was at all, across every way it could occur. The normaliser.
P(H | E)posteriorYour revised belief, after folding the evidence in. The answer you wanted.
One sentence to keep posterior  =  likelihood  ×  prior  ÷  evidence.  Belief in, belief out; the evidence does the updating.

Where it comes from — one step, no magic

You already own everything needed to derive this. From Lesson 1, a conditional probability is a joint probability divided by what you conditioned on. Written two ways for the same overlap of H and E:

P(H∣E) = P(H∩E)P(E) and P(E∣H) = P(H∩E)P(H)

Both have the same numerator, P(H ∩ E) — the fraction of the world where H and E both hold (the “10 people with a dog and a leash” from Lesson 1). Solve the right-hand equation for that shared piece:

P(H∩E) = P(E∣H) ⋅ P(H)

Drop that into the left-hand equation, and Bayes’ theorem falls out — it is nothing more than the definition of conditioning, read in both directions and glued together.1 Not an incantation: a re-arrangement.

What is P(E), really? The denominator P(E) is the evidence happening any way at all — when H is true and when it isn’t: P(E) = P(E|H)·P(H) + P(E|not H)·P(not H). That second term — false alarms from the healthy majority — is the whole reason a rare disease stays unlikely after a positive test. Hold that thought for Lesson 3.

Practice — name the parts, then turn the crank

Here is a worded scenario. Your job is the skill the mission asks for: read prose, pull out the prior and the likelihood, then compute the posterior.

Scenario · the spam filter Across all your mail, 20% is spam. The word “congratulations” appears in 60% of spam, but also in 5% of real mail. A new email contains “congratulations.” What’s the chance it’s spam?

Set H = “the email is spam” and E = “it contains the word.” First, identify the pieces.

Check 1 · find the prior

Which number is the prior, P(H)?

Yes. Before reading a single word, any email has a 20% chance of being spam. That’s the base rate — the prior.
Check 2 · find the likelihood

Which number is the likelihood, P(E | H) — the word given spam?

Right. Given the email is spam, 60% of the time it carries the word. That’s P(E | H).
Check 3 · compute the posterior

Now turn the crank. Out of 100 emails: 20 are spam, and 60% of them — that’s 12 — carry the word. 80 are real, and 5% of them — that’s 4 — carry the word too. Of the emails with the word, what fraction are spam? Give a percentage.

Nailed it — 75%. 12 true hits out of (12 + 4) = 16 emails with the word. The posterior jumped from a 20% prior to 75% because the word is strong evidence. Notice you found it by counting, exactly as the formula prescribes.
Close reading needed. The word appears on 12 spam + 4 real = 16 emails. Of those, 12 are spam. 12 ÷ 16 = 75%. (If you got 60%, you reported the likelihood, not the posterior — the classic Lesson 1 swap.)

The same numbers, through the theorem — confirming the count, not replacing it:

  1. P(H) = 0.20, P(E|H) = 0.60, P(E|not H) = 0.05, P(not H) = 0.80.
  2. P(E) = 0.60·0.20 + 0.05·0.80 = 0.12 + 0.04 = 0.16.
  3. P(H|E) = (0.60 · 0.20) ÷ 0.16 = 0.12 ÷ 0.16 = 0.75. ✓ Same 75%.

Recall — from memory

State Bayes’ theorem and name all four parts.

P(H|E) = P(E|H)·P(H) / P(E). posterior = likelihood × prior ÷ evidence.

In one step, why is it true?

Both P(H|E) and P(E|H) are built from the same shared overlap P(H∩E). Write that overlap as P(E|H)·P(H) and substitute. It’s the definition of conditioning read both ways.

From Lesson 1: why isn’t the posterior just the likelihood?

Because P(H|E) ≠ P(E|H). The likelihood ignores the prior and the false alarms in the denominator. Skipping them is the trap.
