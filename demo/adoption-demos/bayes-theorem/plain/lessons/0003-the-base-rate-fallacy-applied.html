Bayes' theorem · Lesson 3 of 3

The base-rate fallacy, applied

Why a positive result on an accurate test can still mean you're probably fine — and the checklist that proves it every time.

This is the mission: see through the screening paradox without being fooled, using counts you can picture. Lessons 1 and 2 were the build-up. See the mission.

The paradox

A disease affects 1% of people. There's a screening test:

  • If you have the disease, it catches it 99% of the time. (sensitivity — the likelihood)
  • If you don't, it's correct 95% of the time — so it false-alarms 5%. (specificity)

You test positive. Intuition screams "99% accurate, so I'm almost certainly sick." Let's not trust intuition — let's count.

Lead with natural frequencies

Instead of juggling percentages, drop 10,000 people through the test and watch where they land.1 No algebra yet — just bookkeeping.

Now collect everyone who tested positive — both green and blue:

Among the positivesCount
Actually have the disease (true positives)99
Healthy but false-alarmed (false positives)495
Everyone who tests positive594
P(disease∣positive) = 99594 ≈ 0.167 = 16.7%
The shock

A positive result on a 99%-sensitive test, and the real chance you're sick is about 1 in 6. Why? The disease is rare. The test's small 5% false-alarm rate, applied to the huge healthy majority, manufactures 495 false alarms — five times more than the 99 true ones. The base rate (1%) drowns the test's accuracy. Ignoring it is the base-rate fallacy.2

Confirm with the formula

The counts are Bayes' theorem — same numbers, regrouped. From Lesson 2, build the evidence P(positive) then divide:

0.99⋅0.01 (0.99⋅0.01) + (0.05⋅0.99) = 0.00990.0099+0.0495 = 0.00990.0594 ≈ 16.7%

Identical to the count. The 0.0099 and 0.0495 are just the 99 and 495 people, divided by 10,000. Frequencies make it obvious; the formula confirms it.

It's real — and it isn't just medicine

These aren't toy numbers. In a classic study, physicians shown a mammography screening as percentages mostly got it badly wrong; shown the same facts as natural frequencies — "8 of every 1,000 women have breast cancer; 7 of them test positive; 70 of the other 992 also test positive" — most got it right: only 7 of the 77 who test positive actually have cancer, about 9%.1 The same trap drives the prosecutor's fallacy in court and false-alarm floods in airport and spam screening.

The checklist — interleaving everything

Any "positive test, should I worry?" moment. Run these six steps — they fold in the Lesson 1 trap and the Lesson 2 formula:

"Should I worry?" — six steps
  1. Name the hypothesis H (have the disease) and the evidence E (tested positive).
  2. Which number were you handed — the test's P(E | H), or the P(H | E) you actually want? Don't confuse them (Lesson 1).
  3. Find the base rate P(H). If it's small, expect a surprise.
  4. Imagine 10,000 people. How many are true positives? How many false positives?
  5. Posterior = true positives ÷ (true + false) positives.
  6. Sanity-check with Bayes' theorem if you want the decimal exact.

Your turn — practice (new numbers)

Interleaving: a different rare-disease screen. Don't reuse the answer above — rebuild the counts.

A condition affects 0.5% of people. The test is 98% sensitive and 96% specific (4% false alarms). You test positive. Out of 10,000 people, what's the chance you actually have it?

Before computing: roughly, the answer will be…

Right instinct. Rare condition + a few-percent false-alarm rate over a big healthy majority = many false positives. Now get the exact number below.
That's the base-rate fallacy again — 98% is P(positive | disease), not P(disease | positive). The rare base rate pulls the real answer way down.
Specificity isn't the answer — it describes healthy people. You want the fraction of positives who are truly sick. Rework it below.

10,000 people, by the counts:

  • 50 have the condition (0.5%). Of them, 98% test positive → 49 true positives.
  • 9,950 are healthy. Of them, 4% false-alarm → 398 false positives.
  • Total positives = 49 + 398 = 447.
P(condition∣positive) = 49447 ≈0.11=11%

About 1 in 9, despite a 98% test. You identified prior and likelihood (Lesson 2), refused to confuse the inverse (Lesson 1), and led with frequencies (Lesson 3). That's the mission, done.

Your win — and the whole course

You can take any screening result, picture 10,000 people, separate true from false positives, and report the real posterior — then confirm it with Bayes' theorem. A positive on an accurate test no longer fools you, because you always ask first: how rare is the thing to begin with?
