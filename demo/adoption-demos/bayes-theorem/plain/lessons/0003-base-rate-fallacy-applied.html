Lesson 3 of 3

The base-rate fallacy, applied

Why a positive result on a “99% accurate” test can still mean you’re probably fine.

This is the lesson the whole course was built for. You can now flip a conditional (Lesson 1) and run Bayes’ theorem (Lesson 2). Here you’ll point both at the exact situation that fooled you to begin with — a rare condition plus an imperfect test — and watch the “obvious” answer collapse. We’ll do it the way that makes it click: natural frequencies, counting real people, before any algebra.

The scenario

Screening · a rare disease A disease affects 1% of people. The test is good: it catches 90% of people who have the disease (sensitivity). But it also has a 9% false-positive rate — 9% of healthy people test positive anyway.

You test positive. What’s the chance you actually have the disease? Write down your gut answer now.

Most people — including most doctors asked this exact question — answer around 80–90%. The real answer is about to surprise you. When Gigerenzer and colleagues posed equivalent problems, the majority of physicians got them wrong in the probability format.1

Count the people — out of 10,000

Don’t reach for the formula. Take a concrete crowd of 10,000 people and just follow them through.1 This is the natural-frequency method, and it’s the single most reliable way to not get fooled.

Now answer the question you actually care about — P(disease | positive) — by zooming into the positive column, exactly the Lesson 1 move:

P(disease∣positive) = 90 90+891 = 90981 ≈ 9.2%

Look at the positive column visually — 981 people got the scary result, but only the 90 outlined ones are truly sick. The blue flood is false alarms:

truly sick & positive (≈9 in 100 of the positives) healthy but positive — false alarm
The punchline A positive result took you from a 1% chance to roughly a 9% chance. You are still over ten times more likely to be healthy than sick — because the 9% of 9,900 healthy people produces far more positives (891) than the 90% of just 100 sick people (90). The rarity of the disease — the base rate — dominates. Ignoring it is the base-rate fallacy.2

Confirm with the formula from Lesson 2, so you trust the count: P(D|+) = (0.90 · 0.01) / (0.90·0.01 + 0.09·0.99) = 0.009 / 0.0981 ≈ 0.092. Same 9.2%. The formula and the people agree — it always confirms the count, never contradicts it.

Practice — a fresh test, your turn to count

New numbers, same skill. Build the 10,000-person table in your head (or on paper) and read off the positive column.

Scenario · a “99% accurate” screen A condition affects 0.5% of people (50 in 10,000). A test detects 99% of true cases and has a 1% false-positive rate. You test positive.
Check 1 · build the table

Of the 10,000 people, how many are healthy but test positive (false alarms)?

Right. 9,950 healthy × 1% ≈ 100 false positives. Meanwhile 50 sick × 99% ≈ 50 true positives (49.5, round to 50). Hold those two numbers.
Check 2 · read the positive column

So about 50 true positives and 100 false positives. Given your positive result, what’s the chance you actually have the condition? Percentage.

Yes — about 33%. 50 ÷ (50 + 100) = 50 ÷ 150 ≈ 33%. A “99% accurate” test, and a positive still leaves you more likely than not to be fine — because the condition is rare. That’s the fallacy, defused.
Re-read the positive column. Total positives = 50 true + 100 false = 150. Of those, 50 are real: 50 ÷ 150 ≈ 33%. (Answered 99%? That’s the test’s sensitivity — P(+|disease) — the Lesson 1 swap, one more time.)
  1. prior = 0.005, likelihood = 0.99, false-positive = 0.01.
  2. P(+) = 0.99·0.005 + 0.01·0.995 = 0.00495 + 0.00995 = 0.0149.
  3. P(D|+) = 0.00495 ÷ 0.0149 ≈ 0.332 = 33%. ✓

The “should I worry?” checklist

Interleaving Lessons 1–3 into one portable routine. Run it on any “positive test” claim — a medical screen, a fraud alert, a spam flag, a security scanner.

Five questions, in order
  1. Which direction is the quoted number? “Accurate / catches / detects” is almost always P(positive | sick) — the likelihood, not your answer. (Lesson 1.)
  2. What’s the base rate? How rare is the condition in the group being tested? This is the prior, and it’s usually the missing number. (Lesson 2.)
  3. Count out of 10,000. Split into sick vs. healthy, then apply the hit rate and the false-positive rate to each. (Lesson 3.)
  4. Read the positive column. true positives ÷ (true + false positives) = your real risk.
  5. Sanity-check: if false alarms outnumber true hits, a single positive is weak news. Rare condition → stay calm, retest, confirm.

Recall — the whole course, from memory

In one sentence, why can a positive on an accurate test still mean you’re probably fine?

When the condition is rare, the small false-positive rate applied to the huge healthy majority produces more false alarms than the high hit rate applied to the tiny sick group — so most positives are false.

What’s the fastest way to not get fooled?

Count natural frequencies out of a fixed population (e.g. 10,000), then read true-positives ÷ all-positives. The base rate is built in automatically.

Which two quantities does the base-rate fallacy confuse?

P(sick | positive) with P(positive | sick) — by ignoring the prior (base rate) that connects them.
