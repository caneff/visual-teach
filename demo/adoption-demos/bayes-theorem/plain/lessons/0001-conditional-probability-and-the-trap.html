Conditional probability & the trap

The smallest true mental model — and the famous way it gets flipped backwards.

Why this matters for you: your whole problem is mistaking one conditional probability for its reverse. Before any formula, we make that confusion impossible to repeat. See the mission.

Conditioning is just zooming in

A conditional probability, written P(A∣B) and read "the probability of A given B," means one specific thing:

Definition

P(A∣B) is the probability of A, computed only among the cases where B is already true. You throw away every case where B is false, then ask how often A happens in what's left.

That's it. Conditioning on B is zooming into the sub-group where B holds and re-asking the question there. The cleanest way to feel this is to stop thinking in percentages and think in counts over a population.1

A town of 100 people

Picture a town of 100 people. 20 own a dog. Of the whole town, 10 people own a cat. And it happens that 5 people own both.

owns both (5) dog only (15) cat only (5) neither (75)

Now ask the two questions that look the same and aren't:

QuestionZoom into…Answer
P(cat∣dog)
a dog owner — do they have a cat?		 the 20 dog owners 520 = 25%
P(dog∣cat)
a cat owner — do they have a dog?		 the 10 cat owners 510 = 50%

Same 5 people on top both times — the overlap doesn't move. What changes is the denominator: which sub-group you zoomed into. Different group, different answer. That is the whole engine of the confusion.

The asymmetry, stated plainly

P(A∣B) ≠ P(B∣A) in general

Flipping the bar flips the denominator. They are equal only by coincidence — when the two groups happen to be the same size. Reaching for one when you were handed the other has a name: the confusion of the inverse (a.k.a. the inverse fallacy).2

The trap, in the wild

"The test is 99% accurate, so a positive result means I'm 99% likely to be sick." That sentence quietly swaps P(positive∣sick) — a fact about the test — for P(sick∣positive) — the thing you actually want to know. Different denominators. Lesson 3 shows the second number can be far lower than the first.

Your turn — recall & decide

No peeking at the table. Answer from the mental model: which group am I zooming into?

In the town, which statement is true?

Not equal — equality needs equal group sizes, and there are 20 dog owners but only 10 cat owners.
Right. 5⁄20 = 25% versus 5⁄10 = 50%. The bigger denominator (dogs) gives the smaller fraction. The overlap is shared; the group sizes differ.
Check the denominators: dog owners (20) outnumber cat owners (10), so dividing the same 5 by 20 gives the smaller number.

"90% of people with the flu have a fever." A friend has a fever. They conclude there's a 90% chance it's the flu. What went wrong?

That's the trap itself. The statistic conditions on flu; your friend conditions on fever — a different, much larger group (colds, heat, anything).
The number can be perfectly correct and the conclusion still wrong — because it's the answer to the reverse question.
Exactly. The stat is P(fever | flu). Your friend wants P(flu | fever), which zooms into the huge group of feverish people — most of whom don't have the flu. Confusion of the inverse.
Your win this lesson

You can now hear "X% of B are A" and refuse to read it as "X% of A are B." You know the two differ because they zoom into different-sized groups. Next lesson turns that instinct into a formula that gets the reverse number right instead of just flagging the error.
