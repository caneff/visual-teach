Conditional probability and the trap

The smallest true mental model — and the confusion it kills.

Here is the mistake at the centre of this whole course, in one line. A doctor says a screening test is “95% accurate.” You test positive. You conclude you almost certainly have the condition. That conclusion can be badly, life-alteringly wrong — and the reason is a single quiet swap of two things that look the same but are not.

This lesson builds the one idea you need to see the swap coming. No formula yet, no theorem — just counting people. By the end you will be able to say exactly why “the test is right 95% of the time” and “a positive result means you’re sick 95% of the time” are different claims.

Conditioning is just zooming in

A conditional probability, written P(A | B) and read “the probability of A given B,” asks a restricted question: among only the cases where B is true, in what fraction is A also true?1 The vertical bar means “given” — it tells you to throw away every case where B is false and look only at what remains.

The core move Conditioning on B = zoom into the sub-group where B holds, then ask your question inside that smaller world. The bar resets the denominator.

Concrete. Imagine a town of 100 people. We will not use a formula; we will point at dots. Each square is one person.

red = the 12 people who own a dog grey = the other 88

Suppose, in this town:

  • 12 people own a dog.
  • 10 of those dog owners also own a leash. (The other 2 are… optimistic.)
  • 20 people own a leash in total — some leash owners have no dog.

P(leash | dog) — among the 12 dog owners, how many have a leash? That’s 10 of 12 ≈ 83%. Zoom into the dog-owner group; ask about leashes.

P(dog | leash) — among the 20 leash owners, how many have a dog? That’s 10 of 20 = 50%. Different zoom, different denominator, different answer.

Same 10 people, two different fractions The numerator is identical — the 10 folks with both a dog and a leash. What changed is the group you divided by: 12 dog owners versus 20 leash owners. P(A | B) and P(B | A) share a top and swap a bottom. That is the entire trick.

Why the two are not the same

In general, P(A | B) ≠ P(B | A).1 They’re equal only by coincidence (when the two groups happen to be the same size). Treating them as interchangeable has a name — it’s sometimes called confusion of the inverse, or the prosecutor’s fallacy when it shows up in a courtroom.2

Now put the disease back in. The test’s advertised accuracy is a statement of the form:

P(test positive | you are sick) — “given a sick person, the test usually fires.”

But the thing you actually want to know when your own result comes back is the reverse:

P(you are sick | test positive) — “given that the test fired, am I usually sick?”

The trap, named The test sheet quotes P(positive | sick). Your fear lives in P(sick | positive). They are P(A | B) and P(B | A) — the dog and the leash. When the disease is rare, these two numbers can be wildly far apart, and the second is the one that matters for you. Lessons 2 and 3 are about flipping correctly from the first to the second.

Practice — flip the conditional

Don’t just nod along; the swap only becomes visible once you’ve caught it yourself. Answer before revealing.

Check 1 · read the sentence

“Most people who are fluent in Welsh live in Wales.” As a conditional probability, this sentence is closest to —

Yes. Condition on the speakers (the smaller group), then ask where they live: that group is mostly in Wales. The reverse — pick a random Welsh resident, are they a fluent speaker? — is mostly “no.” Same words, opposite truth.
Check 2 · the test sheet

A test’s leaflet says: “In 99% of people who have the disease, this test returns positive.” Which quantity is that?

Right. The leaflet conditions on having the disease, then describes the test. To learn your own risk you must flip it to P(disease | positive) — and that flip needs one more ingredient, how rare the disease is. That’s Lesson 2.
Check 3 · count the sub-group

In a club of 50 people, 30 play chess and 20 of those chess players also play go. What is P( plays go | plays chess ), as a percentage?

(zoom into the 30 chess players)
Exactly. 20 of 30 ≈ 67%. You restricted to the chess players and counted go within them. Note you were not given enough to find P(chess | go) — that would need the total number of go players. Different question, different denominator.
Not yet. Conditioning on “plays chess” means the denominator is the 30 chess players, not 50. Of those 30, the 20 who also play go give 20 ÷ 30 ≈ 67%.

Recall — say it without looking

Cover the screen and answer aloud. Retrieval is what makes this stick.

What does the bar in P(A | B) tell you to do?

Throw away every case where B is false; look only at the sub-group where B holds, and ask about A inside it. The bar resets the denominator.

Why is a test’s “accuracy” not the same as your risk after a positive?

Accuracy is P(positive | sick) — conditioned on being sick. Your risk is P(sick | positive) — conditioned on the positive result. Those are P(A|B) and P(B|A); they differ, often hugely, when the disease is rare.
