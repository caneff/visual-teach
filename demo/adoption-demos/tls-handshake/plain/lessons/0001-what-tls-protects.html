The TLS Handshake · Lesson 1 of 3

What TLS protects, and who's involved

The smallest true mental model: three guarantees, three actors.

Your win for this lesson: say, in one breath, the three things TLS guarantees and the three actors that make it work — so the rest of the handshake has somewhere to land.

You use HTTPS every day. The s is TLS — Transport Layer Security — the protocol that turns a plain, readable TCP connection into a private one. Before we touch a single handshake message, we need the frame the messages hang on: what TLS is trying to achieve, and who is in the room. Get this, and Lessons 2 and 3 are just detail.

The three guarantees

When the padlock appears, the browser is claiming three specific things about the connection to that server. Not vibes — three named properties.1

GuaranteePlain meaningWithout it…
Confidentiality Nobody between you and the server can read the bytes. The café Wi-Fi sees your password.
Integrity Nobody can quietly change the bytes in flight. An attacker rewrites the bank's amount field.
Authentication You're really talking to bank.com, not an impostor. You hand your password to a look-alike.

Confidentiality and integrity are about the channel. Authentication is about the other end — and it is the one most people forget. A perfectly encrypted connection to an attacker is still a disaster. TLS has to prove identity, not just scramble bytes.2

Black box, on purpose Encryption here rests on a keypair: two keys where one undoes what the other does. We treat that as a sealed box for this whole course — we only ever use its properties, never its math. That's all the public-key crypto you need.

The three actors

Three parties produce those guarantees. Two are obvious; the third is the one that makes authentication possible.

💻 Client Your browser or curl. Starts the connection; decides who it trusts.
🖥️ Server The site. Must prove it owns the hostname you asked for.
🏛️ Certificate Authority A third party the client already trusts. Vouches for the server in advance.

What a certificate actually is

A certificate is the object that makes authentication work. Strip away the jargon and it is just three things bound together:

  1. a public key (one half of the server's keypair),
  2. an identity — the hostname, e.g. bank.com,
  3. a signature from a Certificate Authority over the first two.

So a certificate is a public key bound to a hostname, signed by a CA the client already trusts. The CA's signature is the whole point: anyone can claim to be bank.com, but only a real CA will sign that claim — and only after checking the server actually controls the domain.3 Your operating system and browser ship with a built-in list of CAs they trust, so the trust is in place before you ever visit the site.

Why "already trusts" matters Trust can't be bootstrapped mid-conversation — an impostor would just vouch for itself. It has to be established ahead of time, out of band. That pre-loaded list of CAs is what the whole web's authentication leans on. We unpack how that chain is checked in Lesson 3.

That's the model. Guarantees: confidentiality, integrity, authentication. Actors: client, server, CA. Certificate: key + hostname + CA signature. No handshake mechanics yet — just the cast and the goal.
