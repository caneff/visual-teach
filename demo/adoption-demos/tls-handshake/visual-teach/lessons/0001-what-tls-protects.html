TLS 1.3 · Lesson 1 of 3

What TLS protects, and who's involved

The smallest true mental model: three guarantees, three actors, and one thing a certificate actually is. No handshake yet — just the cast.

⏱ ~10 min Needs: HTTP basics Beginner Lesson 1 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Name the three guarantees TLS provides and what each prevents.
  • Identify the three actors and what each one is responsible for.
  • Say what a certificate actually is, in one sentence.
Why this matters for your goal: Before you can debug a certificate error, you need to know what a certificate is for and who vouches for it. This lesson builds that cast of characters; the next two put them in motion.

1The problem TLS solves

Plain HTTP travels over the open internet as readable text. Between your laptop and the server sit dozens of hops — Wi-Fi access points, routers, ISPs, anyone on the same café network. On plain HTTP, every one of them can read what you send, change it in flight, or pretend to be the server you wanted. TLS is the layer that closes all three gaps, turning a plain TCP connection into a secure one — it's the S in HTTPS.

2The three guarantees

Everything TLS does serves exactly three promises. Learn them as a set — each one blocks a specific attack.

Guarantee Plain meaning Without it, an attacker could…
Confidentiality Only the two ends can read the bytes. Read your password as it crosses their network.
Integrity Tampering in flight is detected. Silently change an amount or swap a download.
Authentication You're proven to be talking to the real server. Impersonate the site and collect what you send.
Confidentiality without authentication is a trap. An encrypted connection to an impostor is still an encrypted connection — to the wrong party. That's why authentication (the certificate) carries as much weight as the encryption, and why a cert error is worth stopping for.

3The three actors

Only three parties matter for the web's TLS. Two are on the connection; the third is off to the side, vouching ahead of time.

Client
Your browser / curl
Opens the connection
Server
example.com
Presents a certificate
Certificate Authority
e.g. Let's Encrypt
Signed the cert in advance

Client and server talk live; the CA vouched for the server earlier, out of band.

The key move: the client and the CA never talk during your connection. The CA did its job earlier by signing the server's certificate. Your browser already trusts a fixed set of CAs — they ship in your operating system and browser as the trust store. So when the server presents a cert signed by one of them, the client can verify it offline, on the spot.

4What a certificate actually is

Strip away the jargon and a certificate is one simple binding:

A certificate = a public key, bound to a hostname, signed by a CA the client already trusts. It's the CA saying, in a way anyone can check, "this public key belongs to example.com."

We treat the keys as a black box: a keypair where one key undoes the other, the public half shareable and the private half secret. That single property — plus a CA's signature over the binding — is all you need to follow the rest of the course. The how of the keys agreeing on a secret is Lesson 2; the how of checking the CA's signature is Lesson 3.

6Recall from memory

Produce the answer out loud before you open it — retrieval is what makes it stick.

Name the three guarantees, and one attack each prevents.
Confidentiality — stops eavesdropping (reading your bytes). Integrity — stops tampering (changing them in flight). Authentication — stops impersonation (a fake server).
In one sentence: what is a certificate?
A public key bound to a hostname, signed by a CA the client already trusts — the CA vouching that this key belongs to that name.
Why doesn't your browser phone the CA during the connection?
Because the CA already signed the cert ahead of time, and the CA's root is in the browser's trust store. The client verifies the signature locally, with no live call.

✦ What you earned

TLS makes three promises — confidentiality, integrity, authentication — between two live actors (client, server), backed by a third that signed ahead of time (the CA). A certificate is just a public key bound to a hostname, signed by a trusted CA.
