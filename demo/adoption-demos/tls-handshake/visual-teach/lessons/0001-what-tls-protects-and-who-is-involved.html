TLS Handshake · Lesson 1

What TLS protects, and who's involved

Before any bytes or key math: the three things TLS promises, and the three actors who make those promises true.

⏱ ~10 min Needs: nothing — start here Beginner Lesson 1 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Name the three guarantees TLS provides, and say what each one stops.
  • Name the three actors — client, server, certificate authority — and their jobs.
  • Say what a certificate actually is, in one sentence.
Why this matters for your goal: Every "certificate error" you've ever clicked through is one of these three actors failing to keep one of these three promises. Get the cast and the promises straight now, and the error messages in Lesson 3 stop being noise.

1The three promises

When you load https://bank.example, TLS (the protocol behind the S in HTTPS) wraps your plain HTTP request in three guarantees. Every one of them is something an attacker on the network — sharing your café Wi-Fi, running a router between you and the server — would otherwise be free to do.

Guarantee Plain meaning What it stops
Confidentiality Nobody in the middle can read the bytes. Eavesdropping — sniffing your password off the wire.
Integrity Nobody in the middle can change the bytes undetected. Tampering — flipping "$10" to "$1000" in transit.
Authentication You're really talking to the server you named. Impersonation — a fake server posing as your bank.
Insight — The first two protect the data; the third protects the identity. Encryption alone is worthless without authentication: a perfectly encrypted line to an impostor still hands them your password. Most of this course is really about that third promise — and certificates are how it's kept.

Vendor-neutral definitions: MDN — Transport Layer Security.

2The three actors

Only three parties matter for the whole handshake. Two are obvious; the third is the one most developers never picture.

Client
Your browser / curl
Ships a list of trusted CAs
Server
bank.example
Holds a private key + certificate
Certificate Authority
e.g. Let's Encrypt
Signed the server's certificate

The CA isn't online during your connection — it signed the server's certificate earlier. Your client trusts the CA in advance.

  • Client — starts the connection and does the checking. It ships with a built-in list of certificate authorities it already trusts (your OS / browser trust store).
  • Server — proves who it is. It holds a private key and a matching certificate, and must prove it controls that private key.
  • Certificate Authority (CA) — the trusted third party. It checked the server's identity once and signed its certificate. It is not part of your live connection.

3The black-box keypair

We treat public-key crypto as a sealed box — no RSA or elliptic-curve math in this course. You only need two properties of a keypair (a public key + its matching private key):

The only two rules you need
  • One key undoes the other: what one locks, only the other opens.
  • The public key can be shared with the world; the private key never leaves its owner.

From those two rules alone, a signature works like this: the CA "locks" a summary of the certificate with its private key; anyone with the CA's public key can unlock it and confirm the CA — and only the CA — wrote it. That's all a signature is for our purposes.

4So what is a certificate?

Put the actors and the keypair together and a certificate stops being magic. It is just a signed claim:

A certificate is a public key bound to an identity (a hostname like bank.example), signed by a CA your client already trusts.

That's the whole idea. The server hands the client this certificate during the handshake; the client trusts the contents because it trusts the CA's signature on them. How the client actually verifies that signature — and what happens when it can't — is Lesson 3.

Define a certificate in one sentence.
A public key bound to an identity (a hostname), signed by a certificate authority the client already trusts. See the glossary for the precise terms.

✦ What you earned

TLS keeps three promises — confidentiality, integrity, authentication — among three actors — client, server, CA — and a certificate is just a CA-signed binding of a public key to a hostname.
