Choosing between them

Two shapes, one decision. This lesson turns the pictures from Lesson 1 into a question you can answer in seconds — and then drills it on real situations until the choice is reflex.

⏱ ~12 min Needs: Lesson 1 Beginner Lesson 2 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Apply one question to decide merge vs rebase.
  • Justify the choice in a single sentence.
  • Spot the situation where the choice is forced (the golden rule).
Why this matters for your goal: Your mission is to choose the right command for the situation in front of you, without anxiety. The anxiety comes from having no rule. By the end of this page you'll have exactly one, and you'll have used it on the situations you hit every day.

1The one question

Every other consideration is downstream of a single question about the commits you're about to move:

Have these commits been pushed and possibly used by someone else?
  • Yes → merge — rewriting shared commits breaks everyone who has them.
  • No → rebase is safe — and usually gives a cleaner line worth having.

That's the safety axis, and it's non-negotiable (we'll see why in §4). When rebase is safe, a second, softer question decides whether it's worth it: do you want a clean, linear story (rebase) or an honest record that a branch was integrated (merge)? Taste, team convention, and the size of the change all feed that one.[1]

3Drill: decide and justify

Here's the feedback loop. For each real scenario, pick the command, then check your one-sentence reason against mine. Retrieval under varied scenarios is what builds the reflex — don't skip ahead.[2]

Scenario A. You've made five messy local commits on feature all morning. Before opening a PR you want them to read cleanly on top of the latest main. Nothing is pushed.

Scenario B. A long-running release/2.0 branch is shared by three teammates. You need to pull the latest main into it so the release tracks recent fixes.

Scenario C. Your feature branch is a few commits behind main. You haven't pushed yet and want the newest main changes underneath your work so your PR is up to date.

Scenario D. You already pushed feature and a teammate pulled it to test. Now you'd like to tidy those same commits into a cleaner sequence before review.

4Why "shared" is the hard line

Rebase makes new commits (Lesson 1). If a commit you rebased was already pushed, the original still lives in your teammate's repo. Now two histories claim to be the same branch, and git can't reconcile them cleanly — your teammate gets duplicate commits and ugly conflicts. That's why this isn't a style preference but a rule, stated bluntly in Pro Git:

The golden rule — "Do not rebase commits that exist outside your repository and that people may have based work on."[2] In plain terms: never rebase anything you've already pushed and shared.

Lesson 3 makes this concrete — and shows the undo button for when you slip. For now: private = your call shared = merge.

Primary source Atlassian — "Merging vs. Rebasing" — a well-illustrated walk through these same decisions, with the same golden-rule framing. Read "The Golden Rule of Rebasing" and "Workflow Walkthrough".
