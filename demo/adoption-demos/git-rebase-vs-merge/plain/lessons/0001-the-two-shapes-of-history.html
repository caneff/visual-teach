git merge vs rebase · Lesson 1 of 3

The two shapes of history

Merge and rebase are two answers to one question: how do I combine two lines of work? Before you can choose between them, you need to see what each one does to your commit graph. That picture is the whole mental model — get it, and every later decision falls out of it.

Your win for today: draw both shapes from memory, and explain why rebase produces brand-new commit hashes. No decisions yet — that's Lesson 2.

First, a graph is just dots and arrows

A git history is a graph of commits. Each commit points back to its parent(s). Suppose you branched feature off main, made two commits (C, D), and meanwhile a teammate added a commit (E) to main:

A───B <- the common base │ ├──C──D (feature) your work │ └──E (main) their work

History has diverged. Both branches share the base B, then go their own way. Now you want your feature to include E. Merge and rebase do this differently.

Merge: tie the two lines together with a knot

git merge creates one new merge commit that has two parents — the tip of each branch. Nothing that already existed is changed. Your C and D keep their exact hashes; the real, branching shape is preserved forever.

# on feature
$ git merge main
A───B │ ├──C──D──M (feature) M = merge commit, parents D and E │ ╱ └──E─────╯

M is the knot. The graph now records a true fact: "these two lines of work existed in parallel and were joined here."

Merge in one sentence

Merge adds a commit that joins two histories, preserving the real branching graph exactly as it happened. [Pro Git §3.2]

Fast-forward, the special case: if main hadn't moved (no E), there's nothing to knot together — git just slides the branch pointer forward to your latest commit. No merge commit at all. That's a "fast-forward." It's why some merges create a commit and some don't.

Rebase: pick up your work and replant it

git rebase takes your commits, sets them aside, moves the base of your branch to the tip of the other branch, and then replays your commits one by one on top of the new base.

# on feature
$ git rebase main
A───B───E (main) │ └──C'──D' (feature) replayed on top of E

One straight line. As if you'd started your work after E all along. No knot, no fork.

Why C' and D', not C and D?

A commit's hash is computed from its content and its parent. When rebase replays C onto a new parent (E instead of B), the parent changed, so the hash changes: you get a new commit C'. The old C still exists for now — it's just no longer on the branch. This is the "rewriting history" people warn about. It's not destruction; it's copying onto a new base. [Pro Git §3.6]

The two shapes, side by side

Merge → branching graph

  • Adds a merge commit (two parents)
  • Existing commits keep their hashes
  • History shows what actually happened
  • Graph can get tangled with many merges

Rebase → linear graph

  • Replays your commits onto a new base
  • Produces new commits (new hashes)
  • History is clean, straight, easy to read
  • Rewrites — dangerous if commits are shared
