Regex Cheat Sheet

The core, portable (JavaScript / PCRE-style) syntax — one page, designed to print.

Everything here is the common subset that behaves the same across most engines. Sourced from MDN and regular-expressions.info. For terms, see the glossary.

Match one character

TokenMatchesExample
athat literal charactercat → cat
.any one character (except newline)c.t → cat, cot, c@t
[abc]one of a, b, or c[ch]at → cat, hat
[a-z]one char in a range[0-9] → any digit
[^abc]one char not in the set[^0-9] → any non-digit

Shorthand classes

TokenMeansNegation
\ddigit, [0-9]\D = non-digit
\wword char, [A-Za-z0-9_]\W = non-word
\swhitespace (space, tab, newline)\S = non-whitespace

Capital letter = the negation. Inside [...] the shorthands still work: [\d.] means “a digit or a dot.”

How many times (quantifiers)

TokenRepeats the thing before it…
*0 or more times
+1 or more times
?0 or 1 time (optional)
{3}exactly 3 times
{2,5}between 2 and 5 times
{2,}2 or more times
Greedy vs. lazy Quantifiers are greedy by default — they grab as much as possible, then give back only if the rest of the pattern forces it. Add ? to make one lazy — grab as little as possible. <.*> on <a><b> matches the whole thing; <.*?> matches just <a>.

Positions (anchors) — match between characters

TokenMatches the position…
^at the start of the string (or line, in multiline mode)
$at the end of the string (or line)
\bat a word boundary (edge between \w and non-\w)

Anchors consume no characters. ^cat$ means “the entire string is exactly cat.”

Grouping, alternation, capture

TokenMeans
(…)group — apply a quantifier to the whole, and capture what it matched
(?:…)group only — no capture (use when you just need the parentheses)
a|balternation — match a or b
(ab)+        → "ababab"        captures the last "ab"
(\d{4})-(\d{2})  on "2026-06"   group 1 = "2026", group 2 = "06"
gr(a|e)y     → "gray" or "grey"
https?       → "http" or "https"   (the s is optional)

Escaping

To match a character that has special meaning, put a backslash before it. These need escaping outside a class: . * + ? ( ) [ ] { } ^ $ | \ /

\.     a literal dot          \$     a literal dollar sign
3\.14  matches "3.14"          \(     a literal open paren

Inside [...] most of these lose their power and need no escape — e.g. [.+] matches a literal dot or plus.

Reading order (precedence)

  1. A quantifier binds to the single token just before it — a char, a class, or a (group). ab+ = a then one-or-more b.
  2. Alternation | is the loosest — it splits the whole pattern unless parentheses fence it. ^a|b$ means “starts with a” OR “ends with b”.
