Quantifiers & Anchors

How many, and where. The core skill: turning single-character matches into real patterns.

Why this lesson In Lesson 1 every token matched exactly one position. That's not enough to match “a number” or “the end of a line.” This lesson adds repetition and position — the two ideas that let you write a real pattern for a real task, the heart of your mission.

How many: quantifiers

A quantifier goes after a token and says how many times that token repeats.[1]

TokenRepeats the thing before it…
*0 or more times
+1 or more times
?0 or 1 time (optional)
{3} / {2,5} / {2,}exactly 3 / between 2 and 5 / 2 or more

Remember \d grabbing digits one at a time? Add + and it grabs the whole run — finally, “a number.”

Greedy vs. lazy — why patterns “match too much”

Quantifiers are greedy by default: they grab as much as they possibly can, then hand characters back only if the rest of the pattern can't match otherwise.[2] This is the single most common reason a copied pattern misbehaves. Watch <.*> swallow everything between the first < and the last >:

Add a ? after the quantifier to make it lazy: grab as little as possible. Now each tag matches on its own.

Diagnosis you can now make “My pattern matches too much” almost always means a greedy quantifier ran past where you wanted. Fix: make it lazy (?), or replace . with a tighter class like [^>] that can't cross the boundary.

Where: anchors

Anchors match a position, not a character — they consume no text.[3]

TokenMatches the position…
^at the start of the string
$at the end of the string
\bat a word boundary (edge of a \w run)

The classic combo ^…$ means “the entire string is exactly this.” It's how you validate input — the pattern must match from the first character to the last, with nothing left over.
