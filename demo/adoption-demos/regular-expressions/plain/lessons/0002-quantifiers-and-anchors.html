Quantifiers & anchors

Lesson 1 matched one character at a time. Now: how many times — and how to pin a match to a position rather than a character.

Quantifiers — “how many of the thing before me”

A quantifier attaches to the item right before it (a character, a class, a shorthand) and says how many copies to match.1

QuantifierMeansSo a{q} matches…
*zero or more"", a, aaaa…
+one or morea, aaaa… (not empty)
?zero or one (optional)"" or a
{3}exactly 3aaa
{2,4}between 2 and 4aa, aaa, aaaa
{2,}2 or moreaa, aaa…

Now you can match a number: \d+ is “one or more digits.” A whole word of letters: [a-z]+. An optional sign: -?\d+ matches 42 and -42.

order 1138, ref 7

\d+ grabs each run of digits whole — 1138 and 7 — because + keeps consuming digits as long as it can.

Greedy vs. lazy — why a pattern “matches too much”

Here is the single most common source of regex surprise. Quantifiers are greedy by default: they grab as much as they possibly can, then give characters back only if the rest of the pattern can't otherwise match.2

Say you want the first HTML tag in <b>hi</b> and reach for <.*>. Watch what greed does:

<b>hi</b>

<.*> — greedy. .* races to the end of the line, then backtracks just enough to find a final >. You wanted <b>; you got the whole thing.

Add a ? after the quantifier to make it lazy: match as few as possible, expanding only when forced.

<b>hi</b>

<.*?> — lazy. .*? takes nothing, then grudgingly grows until the first > matches. Now you get just <b>.

Note · the mental test Greedy .* = “take everything, then back off.” Lazy .*? = “take nothing, then inch forward.” When a match swallows too much, suspect a greedy quantifier first.

Anchors — match a position, not a character

Everything so far consumes characters. Anchors are different: they match a zero-width position between characters and assert “you're at the right spot.” They eat nothing.3

AnchorAsserts you're at…
^the start of the string (or line)
$the end of the string (or line)
\ba word boundary — the edge between a \w and a non-\w

Why they matter: \d{5} finds five digits anywhere, including inside id=987654. But ^\d{5}$ matches only if the entire string is exactly five digits — that's the difference between “contains” and “is.” That's how you validate.

90210   9021   902100   id=98765

^\d{5}$ accepts only 90210 — five digits, start to end. The others have too few, too many, or extra characters around them.

The word boundary \b is how you match a whole word and not a fragment. \bcat\b matches cat in “the cat sat” but not the cat inside “category.”

the cat in category

\bcat\b — boundaries on both sides force a standalone word.
