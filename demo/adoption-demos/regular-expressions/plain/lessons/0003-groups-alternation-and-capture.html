Lesson 3 · Applied — the whole skill on one real task

Groups, alternation & capture

Three small additions turn “match characters” into “extract the fields I want.” Then we pull a log line apart, start to finish.

1 · (...) — group, so a quantifier applies to many characters

Parentheses bundle a sub-pattern into one unit. Now a quantifier acts on the whole group, not just the last character.1 Compare:

hahaha vs ha

(ha)+ repeats the pair — matching haha. Without the group, ha+ would mean an h followed by one-or-more a's (haaaa). Grouping changes the unit of repetition.

This is how you match a dotted structure. An IPv4-ish address is four numbers joined by dots — “a number, then (dot + number) three times”: \d{1,3}(\.\d{1,3}){3}.

2 · | — alternation, “this OR that”

The pipe matches the pattern on its left or its right. It's greedy about scope: cat|dog means cat or dog, not ca(t|d)og. Use a group to bound it: gr(a|e)y is the same as the class gr[ae]y, but | shines when the alternatives are whole words.1

GET /a   POST /b

GET|POST|PUT|DELETE matches whichever HTTP method appears. For a fixed set of words, alternation is clearer than a character class.

3 · Capturing vs. non-capturing groups

A plain (...) does two jobs at once: it groups and it captures — the engine remembers what that group matched and hands it back as a numbered piece (group 1, group 2, …) so your code can pull it out.2 Captures are read left-to-right by the position of each opening parenthesis.

2024-10-10

(\d{4})-(\d{2})-(\d{2}) on 2024-10-10. The whole match is the date; group 1=2024, group 2=10, group 3=10. The underlined parts are what you'd read out of the result.

Sometimes you need the grouping (for a quantifier or alternation) but you don't want to capture it — it's just noise in your results. Make it non-capturing with (?:...). Same grouping behaviour, no captured slot.2

Note · keep your capture slots meaningful In (\d{1,3})(?:\.\d{1,3}){3} the first group captures the leading octet; the repeated (?:…) does the joining work without cluttering your results with a throwaway group. Capture what you want to read; use (?:…) for everything else.

Putting it together — read the fields out of a log line

This is the mission in one task. Here is a common access-log line:

192.168.1.5 - - [10/Oct/2024:13:55:36] "GET /index.html" 200

We want three fields: the client IP, the HTTP method, and the status code. Build the pattern field by field — exactly the interleaving of Lessons 1–3:

FieldSub-patternLesson
client IP(\d{1,3}(?:\.\d{1,3}){3})classes + groups
HTTP method(GET|POST|PUT|DELETE)alternation
status code(\d{3})quantifier {3}

Stitched together with the literal punctuation of the line between them:

^(\d{1,3}(?:\.\d{1,3}){3}).*"(GET|POST|PUT|DELETE) .*" (\d{3})$
192.168.1.5 - - [10/Oct/2024:13:55:36] "GET /index.html" 200

The whole line matches; the three underlined captures are IP, method, status — groups 1, 2, 3. Note the lazy-vs-greedy care: the .* bridges are fine here because the " and trailing $\,(\d{3}) pin the ends.
