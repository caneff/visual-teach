Groups, Alternation & Capture

Applied: bundle, choose, and extract — then pull the fields out of a real log line.

Why this lesson This is the payoff for your mission. You'll combine everything from Lessons 1 and 2 into one real task: reading a concrete string and pulling structured fields out of it — no copy-paste, no guessing.

Grouping: parentheses bundle a sub-pattern

Parentheses (…) turn several tokens into one unit, so a quantifier (or an |) applies to the whole bundle.[1] Without the group, ab+ means “a then one-or-more b.” With it, (ab)+ means “one-or-more ab pairs.”

Alternation: choose between options

The | operator means “match the left side or the right.” Fence it in a group to control exactly what it chooses between.[2] Below, the captured choice (the tinted part) is the one letter that differed:

Precedence trap | is the loosest-binding operator. So ^cat|dog$ does not mean “cat or dog on its own line” — it means “starts with cat” OR “ends with dog.” To get the intended meaning, group it: ^(cat|dog)$.

Capturing: parentheses also remember

Every (…) group captures — it remembers the text it matched, so you can pull it out afterward. Groups are numbered left-to-right by their opening paren: group 1, group 2, group 3.[1] Here a single pattern splits a date into three fields:

The applied checklist

When you face a real “extract / validate this string” task, work it in this order — it's just Lessons 1→2→3 in sequence:

  1. Describe the target out loud. “A 4-digit year, a dash, two digits…”
  2. Pick the class for each piece. Literal? \d? a [set]? (Lesson 1)
  3. Add the count. How many of each — +, {4}, optional ?? (Lesson 2)
  4. Anchor if it must be the whole string. Wrap in ^…$ to validate. (Lesson 2)
  5. Wrap each field you want to extract in (…). Those become your captured fields.
