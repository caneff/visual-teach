Regular expressions · Lesson 2

Quantifiers and anchors

Lesson 1 matched one character at a time. Now you say how many times — and pin a match to a position instead of a character. This is the core skill.

⏱ ~15 min Needs: Lesson 1 Beginner Lesson 2 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Repeat a piece with *, +, ?, {n}, and {n,m}.
  • Explain why greedy .* "matches too much" and fix it with lazy .*?.
  • Anchor a match to the start ^, end $, or a word boundary \b.
  • Given a target string and a goal, write the pattern from scratch.
Why this matters for your goal: "Matches too much" and "matches too little" are the two ways a pasted pattern betrays you. Both live here — in how many times a piece repeats, and in where the match is allowed to begin and end. Master this and you can fix most broken regexes on sight. See your mission.

1A quantifier attaches to the thing before it

A quantifier is a suffix that says how many times the immediately preceding piece may repeat — a literal, a ., or a character class. This is the mental shift from Lesson 1: instead of writing \d\d\d\d, you write \d{4}.

Quantifier Means "the previous piece…" Example
* zero or more times colou*r → "color", "colour", "colouur"
+ one or more times \d+ → one or more digits
? zero or one time (optional) colou?r → "color" or "colour"
{n} exactly n times \d{4} → exactly four digits
{n,m} between n and m times \d{2,4} → two to four digits
Edge cases of { }: {n,} means "n or more" (no upper bound); {0,1} is just ?; {0,} is *; {1,} is +. The three symbols are shorthand for the common counts.
pattern /\d{4}-\d{2}-\d{2}/
subject logged on 2026-06-25 at noon

Four digits, hyphen, two digits, hyphen, two digits. Counted quantifiers turn a vague "some numbers and dashes" into a precise date shape.

2Greedy by default — why .* eats too much

Here's the single most common regex surprise. Quantifiers are greedy: they grab as much as they possibly can, then give back only the minimum needed to let the rest of the pattern match. Watch ".*" try to match a quoted word:

pattern /".*"/
subject say "hi" and "bye" now

Too much. You wanted "hi". But .* grabbed everything to the end, then backed up to the last quote it could find — swallowing hi" and "bye in one match. Greedy means "as much as possible."

Add a ? after a quantifier to make it lazy: match as few as possible, then let the rest of the pattern proceed. Same pattern, one extra character:

pattern /".*?"/
subject say "hi" and "bye" now

Just right. Lazy .*? stops at the first closing quote, so each quoted span is its own match. The fix for "matches too much" is almost always: make the quantifier lazy, or make the class narrower (e.g. [^"]* — "anything but a quote").

Two fixes, one disease. Over-matching has two cures: make the quantifier lazy (.*?), or make the class narrower so it physically can't cross the boundary ([^"]*). The narrow-class fix is often faster and clearer — prefer it when there's an obvious "stop" character.

3Anchors — matching a position, not a character

Everything so far consumes characters. Anchors are different: they match a position between characters and consume nothing. They're how you say "at the very start," "at the very end," or "at the edge of a word."

Anchor Matches the position…
^ at the start of the string (or line)
$ at the end of the string (or line)
\b at a word boundary — between a \w and a \W (or string edge)
Two different carets. ^ at the start of the pattern is the start anchor. ^ inside [...] negates the class (Lesson 1). Same symbol, position decides the meaning.

Anchors stop a pattern from matching in the wrong place. Compare cat with \bcat\b against the same text:

pattern /\bcat\b/
subject the cat in the catalog

The \b on each side demands a word boundary, so "cat" matches only as a whole word — the "cat" buried in "catalog" is skipped, because there's no boundary between "cat" and "alog".

And ^…$ wrapped around a pattern means "the whole string must be exactly this" — the workhorse of input validation:

pattern /^\d{3}-\d{4}$/
subject 867-5309

With anchors, 555-1234 ext 9 would fail — the $ insists nothing follows the four digits. Without anchors, the same junk string would match the part that fits and pass. Anchors are how validation says "all of it, nothing else."

4The core loop: write the pattern

This is the skill the whole course is built toward. For each task below, a target string and a goal are given. Write the pattern yourself first — out loud or on paper — then reveal the answer and compare. Producing it from memory is the effortful retrieval that makes it stick; reading the answer first teaches you almost nothing.

Paste your attempt into regex101 against the target before you reveal — closing your own feedback loop is the point.

Goal: match a 4-digit year. Target: born 1991 here.
\d{4} — exactly four digits. (If you must reject 5-digit runs, anchor or bound it; for a bare extract, \d{4} is fine.)
Goal: match a US ZIP, optionally with a +4 suffix. Targets: 90210 and 90210-1234.
\d{5}(-\d{4})? — five digits, then an optional group of a hyphen and four digits. The ? makes the whole (-\d{4}) optional. (Groups are Lesson 3 — you just previewed them.)
Goal: validate that the entire input is lowercase letters only. Target: hello (and reject hello1).
^[a-z]+$ — start, one or more lowercase letters, end. The anchors are what make it reject hello1: without $, the pattern would happily match the hello and ignore the 1.
Goal: grab the text inside the first pair of parentheses. Target: f(x) + g(y) → you want x.
\([^)]*\) — a literal (, then "anything that isn't a )" repeated, then a literal ). The \( and \) are escaped because parentheses are metacharacters (Lesson 3). The narrow class [^)]* is the greedy-fix from §2 — it physically can't cross into g(y).
