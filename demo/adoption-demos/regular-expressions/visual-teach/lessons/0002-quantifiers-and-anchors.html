Regular Expressions · Lesson 2

Quantifiers and anchors

Lesson 1 matched one character at a time. Now: how many times, and where. This is the core skill — and where most copy-pasted patterns go wrong.

⏱ ~15 min Needs: Lesson 1 Beginner Lesson 2 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Repeat a piece with * + ? and {n} / {n,m}.
  • Explain why a greedy .* "matches too much," and fix it with a lazy .*?.
  • Anchor a match to a position with ^, $, and word boundary \b.
  • Given a target string and a goal, write the pattern.
Why this matters for your goal: "the pattern grabs too much" is the single most common reason a copy-pasted regex misbehaves. Once you see why greedy matching does that, you stop tweaking blindly and start fixing on purpose.

1Quantifiers — how many times

A quantifier attaches to the thing right before it and says how many copies to match. The "thing before" is usually one character or one class — so \d+ means "one or more digits," not "the whole string of stuff."

a*
zero or more a — matches even if there are none
a+
one or more a — needs at least one
a?
zero or one a — makes it optional
a{3}
exactly three a
a{2,4}
between two and four a
a{2,}
two or more a (no upper bound)

Put it on a class to match a run. \d+ grabs each whole number; \w+ grabs each whole word. Try both on the sample:

Try \d+, then \w+, then \d{4}

Order 1742 shipped on 2026-06-26 to bay 7.
The quantifier binds to one piece. go+ is "g then one-or-more o" (matches go, goo) — not "one-or-more go." To repeat a group you need (go)+ — that's Lesson 3.

2Greedy vs. lazy — why .* eats too much

Quantifiers are greedy by default: they grab as much as they possibly can, then give characters back only if the rest of the pattern fails. That's the secret behind "it matched way more than I wanted."

Classic trap: you want each <tag>, so you write <.*>. The greedy .* races to the end of the line, then backtracks to the last > — swallowing everything between the first and last bracket as one match:

Greedy: one giant match. Now add a ? → <.*?>

<a> links to <b> and <c>

Add a ? after the quantifier to make it lazy: it grabs as little as possible, so <.*?> stops at the first > and you get each tag separately.

PatternBehaviourOn <a> … <c>
<.*>greedy — most possibleone match, the whole line
<.*?>lazy — least possiblethree matches, one per tag
<[^>]*>"anything but >"three matches — often the sharpest fix
The deeper fix is usually a class, not laziness. [^>]* says "as many non-> characters as possible" — it physically can't cross a >, so it can't overshoot. Reach for "negate the boundary character" before reaching for lazy. Russ Cox explains why greedy backtracking can get slow ↗

3Anchors — match a position, not a character

Everything so far consumed characters. Anchors are different: they match a position between characters and consume nothing. They're how you say "at the start," "at the end," or "at a word edge."

^
start of the string (or line, in multiline mode)
$
end of the string (or line)
\b
a word boundary — the edge between a \w and a non-\w
\B
a position that is not a word boundary

Because they're zero-width, the lab marks them with a red caret | instead of a highlight. Try the bare boundary \b to see every word edge, then the practical \bcat\b to match only the standalone word:

Try \b alone (carets), then \bcat\b

cat category scatter the cat.
This is how you stop matching inside words. \bcat\b skips category and scatter because those cats aren't bounded by word edges — exactly the "matches too much" problem, solved with a position instead of a character.

4Recall — produce it from memory

Answer before revealing — the effort is the point.

What's the difference between a* and a+?
a* matches zero or more a (can match nothing); a+ needs at least one.
You wrote ".*" to grab a quoted string but it swallowed two of them. Why, and what's the fix?
Greedy .* ran to the last quote, spanning both strings. Make it lazy — ".*?" — or, sharper, forbid the quote: "[^"]*".
How is \b different from a space?
A space is a character the engine consumes. \b is a zero-width position at a word edge — it also matches at the very start/end of the string and next to punctuation, where there's no space at all.

On the text aaa, how many characters does the greedy a+ match in its first match?

5Your win — write the pattern for the goal

Three targets. For each, write a pattern that matches exactly the highlighted goal — no more, no less. The lab tells you the moment you nail it.

A. Match each price token, including the $ — the values are $19, $4, and $1299.

Goal: $19, $4, $1299

Prices: $19, $4 and $1299 today only.
Hint / answer for A
$ is a special character (the end anchor), so escape it: \$\d+ — a literal dollar sign followed by one or more digits.

B. Pull out each quoted string, quotes included — the goals are "hi" and "bye". (Beware the greedy trap!)

Goal: "hi" and "bye" — as two separate matches

she said "hi" then "bye" quietly
Hint / answer for B
A greedy ".*" grabs "hi" then "bye" as one match. Go lazy — ".*?" — or forbid the quote — "[^"]*". Both give you the two strings.

C. Match only the standalone word is — not the is hidden inside this or his.

Goal: the one standalone "is"

this is his list
Hint / answer for C
Bound it on both sides with word boundaries: \bis\b. The is in this and his has a letter on one side, so no boundary — they're skipped.
