Groups, alternation, and capture

The applied payoff: bundle pieces into a chunk, match "this or that," and pull the fields you care about straight out of a real log line.

⏱ ~16 min Needs: L1 + L2 Intermediate Lesson 3 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Group pieces with (…) so a quantifier or | applies to the whole chunk.
  • Match alternatives with |, and scope them with a group.
  • Tell a capturing group (…) from a non-capturing (?:…).
  • Use captures to extract the fields out of a concrete string — a log line.
Why this matters for your goal: reading and writing patterns is the skill; extraction is what you'll actually do with it at work. This lesson turns everything from Lessons 1–2 into one real task: hand the engine a messy line and get back clean fields.

1Grouping with (…)

Parentheses bundle several pieces into one unit. Now a quantifier can apply to the whole chunk, not just the last character. Compare ab+ ("a then one-or-more b") with (ab)+ ("one-or-more ab"):

Try (ab)+, then ab+ — watch the difference

ababab abbb ab a

Grouping is also how you repeat a structured chunk. A dotted-quad like an IPv4 address is "three of (digits-and-a-dot), then digits": (\d{1,3}\.){3}\d{1,3}.

Grouping lets you repeat a whole shape

from 10.0.0.1 via 192.168.1.255 ok

2Alternation | — this or that

A pipe means "match the thing on the left or the thing on the right." On its own it splits the entire pattern, so you almost always wrap the choice in a group to control its reach.

cat|dog
the whole word cat, or the whole word dog
gr(a|e)y
gr, then a or e, then y — matches gray and grey
(GET|POST|PUT)
any one of three HTTP methods

Scope the choice with a group

gray and grey, but not groy or graey
Scope matters. Without the group, gra|ey means "gra or ey" — two unrelated alternatives, not the single-letter choice you meant. The group keeps the | on a leash.

3Capturing vs. non-capturing

A plain group does two jobs: it bundles, and it captures — the engine remembers the text it matched as a numbered group (1, 2, 3… left to right by opening paren). Captures are how you extract pieces of a match.

Below, each capture group is painted its own colour. The pattern (\w+)@(\w+) remembers the name and the host separately:

Two capture groups — see the legend appear

ada@math sam@lab grace@navy

Sometimes you want a group only to bundle — to apply a quantifier or an alternation — without spending a capture slot on it. Make it non-capturing with (?:…):

(?:ab)+
repeat ab, but don't capture it
(\d{4})-(?:\d{2})-(\d{2})
capture year and day; group the month but skip capturing it
Why bother? Non-capturing groups keep your capture numbers clean — group 1 stays the field you actually want, instead of being some structural group you only added to attach a +. Clearer patterns, and faster on big inputs.

4Applied — read a log line, then build the pattern

Here's the real task. This line interleaves everything: literals, classes, quantifiers, and now capture.

2026-06-26 ERROR auth: login failed

Read it left to right and pick out the shape of each field:

\d{4}-\d{2}-\d{2}
the date — four digits, dash, two, dash, two
\w+
the level — a run of word characters (ERROR)
\w+
the component — another word run (auth), before the colon

Wrap each field you want to keep in a capturing group, and put the literal spaces and colon between them:

Build it up: (\d{4}-\d{2}-\d{2}) + a space + (\w+) + a space + (\w+) + a literal :.

5An applied checklist — build any extraction pattern

This is the routine to reuse every time you face a new string. Work it top to bottom; your progress is saved.

  • Write out one concrete example line Paste a real sample. You're matching this, not an idea of the data.
  • Name the fields you want to keep Date? Level? Each one becomes a capturing group (…).
  • Pick the tightest class for each field Prefer \d or [^:]+ over a loose .* — tight classes can't overshoot (Lesson 2).
  • Write the literal glue between fields The spaces, dashes, colons that actually appear. Escape specials like . and $.
  • Test against the sample — and a near-miss Confirm it captures the right fields, and that a slightly-wrong line fails. Then you're done.

6Recall — produce it from memory

Say it before you reveal it.

What two jobs does a plain (…) group do?
It bundles pieces into one unit (so a quantifier or | applies to all of them) and it captures the matched text as a numbered group.
When would you reach for (?:…) instead of (…)?
When you need the grouping (to repeat a chunk or scope a |) but don't want it to consume a capture number — keeping your real fields as groups 1, 2, 3.
Why does ^cat|dog$ probably not mean what someone intended?
| splits the whole pattern, so it reads as "^cat OR dog$" — not "the line is exactly cat or dog." For that, group it: ^(cat|dog)$.

In (\d{4})-(?:\d{2})-(\d{2}) matched against 2026-06-26, what is captured as group 2?

7Your win — extract the fields

The capstone. Write a pattern with three capturing groups that pulls the date, the level, and the component out of the log line. The lab checks your captured fields, not just the match — it turns green when groups 1–3 are exactly 2026-06-26, ERROR, auth.

Capture date, level, component — in that order

2026-06-26 ERROR auth: login failed
Stuck? Reveal the build (try the checklist first!)
(\d{4}-\d{2}-\d{2}) (\w+) (\w+): — date group, a literal space, level group, a space, component group, then the literal colon that ends the component. You could tighten the component to ([^:]+) to make "everything up to the colon" explicit. Group 1 is the date, group 2 the level, group 3 the component.
That's the mission. You just read a messy line, named its fields, chose tight classes, and wrote a capturing pattern that extracts them — no copy-paste, no guessing. That's the whole skill, end to end.
