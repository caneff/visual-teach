Keyboard, intervals & scales — cheat sheet

The compressed essence of the course, built for quick reference and clean printing. Everything is counted on the keyboard, in semitones.

1The keyboard map

Seven white-key letters repeat every octave; a black key sits between most of them. Landmark: the white key just left of a pair of two black keys is always C. There is no black key between E–F or B–C.

  • Semitone (half step) = one key to the very next key.
  • Whole step = two semitones (skip one key).
  • Octave = 12 semitones; same letter, higher register.

3The major scale recipe

From any root, apply this fixed pattern of steps to pick out the seven notes of the key, landing back on the root an octave up:

Step patternW – W – H – W – W – W – H
Semitones from root0 · 2 · 4 · 5 · 7 · 9 · 11 · (12)
Scale degrees1 · 2 · 3 · 4 · 5 · 6 · 7 · (8 = octave)
Why accidentals appear. Only C major needs no black keys — its half steps (E→F, B→C) fall where there's no black key. Every other root needs a sharp or flat somewhere to keep the same W–W–H–W–W–W–H shape.

5Glossary

Semitone / half stepThe smallest distance — one key to the next. 1 semitone.
Whole stepTwo semitones; skips one key.
Octave12 semitones; the same letter name, higher or lower.
Octave equivalenceNotes an octave apart share a name and sound "the same, higher/lower."
IntervalThe distance between two notes, measured in semitones.
RootThe starting note a scale (or chord) is built from.
Major scaleSeven notes built by W–W–H–W–W–W–H from the root.
Sharp (♯) / Flat (♭)Raise / lower a note by one semitone.
EnharmonicOne key, two names (e.g. C♯ = D♭).
ConsonanceHow blended/stable an interval sounds; octaves and fifths are most consonant.
