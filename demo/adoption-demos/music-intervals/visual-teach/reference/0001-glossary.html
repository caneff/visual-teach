Twelve Notes · Reference

Glossary

The course's vocabulary, defined once and used consistently. Print-friendly.

Every term below is used the same way in every lesson. When a lesson italicises a term, it's defined here.

TermDefinition
Octave The distance from a note to the next note of the same name, higher or lower. Spans 12 semitones.
Semitone (half step) The smallest step in the system — one key to its nearest neighbour, white or black. The atom of all distance.
Whole tone (whole step) Two semitones; skips exactly one key between the two notes.
Pitch class A note name regardless of octave — all the C's are one pitch class. There are 12.
Octave equivalence The principle that the same 12 names repeat in every octave; C an octave up is "the same note, higher."
Sharp (♯) One semitone above a natural note. C♯ is the key just above C.
Flat (♭) One semitone below a natural note. D♭ is the key just below D — the same key as C♯.
Enharmonic Two names for the same key (e.g. C♯ / D♭). Which spelling you use depends on the key.
Interval The distance between two notes, measured as a number of semitones.
Consonance / Dissonance How smooth/"resolved" (consonant) or tense/"unresolved" (dissonant) two notes sound together. The octave and perfect fifth are strongly consonant.
Major third An interval of 4 semitones; bright-sounding, the bottom of a major chord.
Perfect fifth An interval of 7 semitones; the most stable, open distance after the octave.
Scale An ordered set of notes within an octave, defined by a pattern of intervals.
Major scale Seven notes built by the step pattern W-W-H-W-W-W-H from a chosen root.
Root / Tonic The starting note of a scale or key; the note the key is named after (e.g. the C in C major).
Scale degree A note's numbered position in a scale: 1 (root), 2, 3 … up to 7, then the octave.
Key The set of notes (and home note) a piece is built around — in this course, the notes of a major scale.

The 12 pitch classes, in order

C · C♯/D♭ · D · D♯/E♭ · E · F · F♯/G♭ · G · G♯/A♭ · A · A♯/B♭ · B → (back to C)

No black key sits between E–F or B–C; those pairs are a single semitone apart already.
