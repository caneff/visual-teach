Intervals & Major Scales — Cheat Sheet

One page to glance at. Prints clean. Everything is counting on the twelve-note grid.

Count the grid

Distance = number of semitones = keys stepped (white and black). Count the moves, not the starting key.

Numbers are semitones from the left-hand C. C♯/D♭ = 1, D = 2, …, the next C = 12.

Intervals to know

The major scale recipe

W  —  W  —  H  —  W  —  W  —  W  —  H

2 · 2 · 1 · 2 · 2 · 2 · 1 semitones — same from any root.

Worked keys

KeyNotes (degrees 1–7, then 8)Accidental
C majorC · D · E · F · G · A · B · Cnone (all white)
G majorG · A · B · C · D · E · F♯ · Gone sharp
F majorF · G · A · B♭ · C · D · E · Fone flat
D majorD · E · F♯ · G · A · B · C♯ · Dtwo sharps

Two rules that resolve every "sharp or flat?" question

1. Apply the recipe in semitones first — find the key.
2. Then spell it so each letter A–G is used exactly once. That decides sharp vs flat (G major → F♯, never G♭).

Self-check (cover the answers)

  • C → G is how many semitones, and what's it called? → 7, a perfect fifth.
  • Spell A major. → A · B · C♯ · D · E · F♯ · G♯ · A (three sharps).
  • Why does the major scale's half steps fall on E–F and B–C in C major? → Those white pairs are already one semitone apart, matching the recipe's H steps (3→4, 7→8).
