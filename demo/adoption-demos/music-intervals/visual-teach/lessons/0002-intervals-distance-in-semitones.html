Intervals: Distance in Semitones

An interval is just how far apart two notes are — counted on the grid you already know.

In Lesson 1 you learned the grid: twelve semitones, repeating. Now the core skill of the whole course. An interval is the distance between two notes, measured as a number of semitones.1 That's it — count the keys between the two notes (white and black alike) and you have the interval. This single skill is what lets you stop guessing.

How to count, exactly

Put your finger on the lower note. Step up one key at a time — counting each key you land on — until you reach the higher note. The number of steps is the interval in semitones. Do not count the starting key as one; count the moves, not the keys.

Worked example

From C up to G: C→C♯ (1), C♯→D (2), D→D♯ (3), D♯→E (4), E→F (5), F→F♯ (6), F♯→G (7). Seven semitones. That interval has a name — a perfect fifth — and it's the most stable, "open"-sounding distance after the octave.

The five intervals to know by name

You don't need all of them yet — just these five everyday distances. Notice how consonance (how smooth or "resolved" two notes sound together) tracks loosely with these familiar distances.

SemitonesNameFeel
1Half stepTense, adjacent — wants to move
2Whole stepThe basic "scale" step
4Major thirdBright, happy — bottom of a major chord
7Perfect fifthOpen and stable — power-chord sound
12OctaveSame note name, fully resolved

The whole step (2) and half step (1) are the two you'll lean on hardest — in Lesson 3 the entire major scale is built out of just those two.

Practice: the interval trainer

This is the heart of the lesson — a tight feedback loop. Two keys light up: the bottom note in red, the top in blue. First count the semitones, then name the interval. You get feedback instantly. Aim for a streak where you don't have to count slowly — that's the skill becoming automatic.

Your win this lesson

Given any two notes on the keyboard you can count the semitones between them and name the common intervals. You're measuring distance on purpose — the exact thing scales are made of.

Why this matters for the mission

A scale, a chord, a melody that "sounds right" — all of them are just specific patterns of intervals. Once distance is something you measure instead of feel for, you can reproduce and transpose those patterns anywhere. Next lesson turns two of these intervals into a recipe for any major scale.
