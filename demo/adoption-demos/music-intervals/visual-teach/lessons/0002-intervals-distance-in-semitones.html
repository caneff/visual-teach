Intervals: distance in semitones

An interval is just the gap between two notes, measured in semitones. Learn to count it, name the five that matter, and the rest of theory opens up.

⏱ ~15 min Needs: Lesson 1 complete Beginner Lesson 2 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Count the distance between any two keys in semitones.
  • Name the five everyday intervals by their semitone count.
  • Recognise the rough "feel" — tense vs. stable — of each.
Why this matters for your goal: "why do these notes sound right together?" is really a question about intervals. Once you can measure the gap, you can stop guessing and start choosing notes on purpose — and the major scale in Lesson 3 is built entirely from these distances.

1An interval is a count of semitones

From Lesson 1, one semitone is one key to the next. An interval is simply how many semitones lie between two notes. The golden rule: count keys, not letters — every key, black and white, counts as one step.

The classic beginner trap. Counting letter names (C, D, E…) skips over the black keys and gives the wrong distance. Always count the actual keys you pass. C up to E is four keys (C→C♯→D→D♯→E), not two.

2The five intervals to know

You don't need all of them yet — these five do most of the work, and each has a recognisable character. The "From C" column tells you which key you land on starting from C; try each on the keyboard below the table.

Interval Semitones From C → Feel
Half step 1 C♯ Tense, leaning — wants to resolve
Whole step 2 D Open, neutral, easy
Major third 4 E Bright and sweet — the "happy" colour
Perfect fifth 7 G Strong, stable, pure
Octave 12 C The same note, higher

Click the green root and then any key on this keyboard — the readout tells you the distance in semitones, so you can feel how counting works.

Consonance, in one line. The octave and perfect fifth are the most blended, stable-sounding gaps; the major third is sweet; the half step is the most tense. That spectrum — stable to tense — is the raw material every chord and melody plays with.

4Make it stick

Recall first, reveal second — mixing these questions up is what cements the counts.

How many semitones is a perfect fifth?
Seven. From C that lands you on G.
Why is C up to E four semitones, not two?
Because you count keys, not letters: C→C♯→D→D♯→E passes four keys. The two black keys in between are easy to skip if you count letter names.
