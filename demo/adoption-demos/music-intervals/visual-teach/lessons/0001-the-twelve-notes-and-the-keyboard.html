The twelve notes and the keyboard

The smallest true mental model of Western music: twelve equal steps that repeat forever, laid out on a keyboard you can read at a glance.

⏱ ~10 min Needs: nothing — start here Beginner Lesson 1 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Find and name any white key on a labelled keyboard.
  • Explain why a note name repeats higher and lower (octave equivalence).
  • Say what a semitone is and why twelve of them make an octave.
Why this matters for your goal: you can't measure the distance between two notes until you can see the ruler. This lesson is the ruler — the twelve repeating steps every interval and scale is later counted on.

1The map: a keyboard of repeating names

Western music uses just seven letter names for the white keys — A B C D E F G — and then starts over. On a piano they run left (low) to right (high). The black keys cluster in groups of two and three; that visual pattern is your landmark for finding any note without counting from the end every time. The note immediately to the left of a group of two black keys is always C.

Click any key below. The readout names it — and notice that the same letters come round again and again as you move right.

Tip: tap the leftmost C, then the next C to its right — same name, higher sound.

Octave equivalence — when you reach the next key with the same letter, you've travelled one octave. It sounds like "the same note, higher." That's why we reuse the seven letters instead of inventing new names forever.

2The black keys, briefly

The black keys are the in-between notes. Each one borrows the name of a neighbour plus a symbol: a sharp (♯) means "one step higher," a flat (♭) means "one step lower." So the black key just right of C is C♯ — and that very same key can also be called D♭. You don't need to memorise these yet; just notice every black key sits between two white ones, and that there's no black key between E–F or B–C.

Count the keys in one octave. From any C up to the next C, you pass 7 white keys and 5 black keys before you arrive — and the arrival C is step number twelve. That's the whole system: twelve equal keys, then it repeats.

3A semitone is one key to the next

The distance from any key to the very next key — white or black, no skipping — is one semitone (also called a half step). It is the smallest move in this system. Stack twelve semitones and you land on the same letter, one octave up. Let's prove it by walking it.

Walk the octave. Click the leftmost C, then click each next key to the right — one at a time, no skips — until you reach the next C. Watch the step count.

Step count: 0 — start on C.

4Make it stick

Answer each from memory before revealing — the effort is what builds lasting recall.

How many semitones are there in one octave?
Twelve. From any key to the next key with the same letter name, you move through twelve semitones.
You're on a key. What's the quickest way to find C nearby?
Find a group of two black keys; the white key just to the left of that pair is C.

Two keys have the same letter name, one higher than the other. What is the relationship between them called?

✦ What you earned

You can read a labelled keyboard: name the white keys off the two-and-three black-key landmark, see that the seven letters repeat every octave, and you know a semitone is one key to the next, twelve to an octave.

I'm your teacher — ask me anything

If the black-key naming felt slippery, or you want me to quiz you on random keys until naming them is automatic, just say so. A couple of openers:

Why do C♯ and D♭ name the same key? Drill me: name 10 random keys. Why are there no black keys between E–F and B–C?
