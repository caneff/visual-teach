The Twelve Notes and the Keyboard

The smallest true mental model: an octave is twelve equal steps that repeat forever.

You already make sounds you like on your instrument. The reason this course can get you from guessing to building on purpose (your mission) is that all of Western music sits on one simple grid. Learn the grid and everything later — intervals, scales, keys — is just counting on it. This lesson installs the grid. No interval names, no scales yet. One win: read any note on the keyboard and know why its name comes back around.

One octave = twelve equal semitones

Pick any note and the next note that sounds "the same but higher" is its octave. The space between them is divided into twelve equal steps. Each smallest step is a semitone (also called a half step) — the smallest move in this system.1 There is nothing between two adjacent keys; the semitone is the atom.

The piano keyboard is simply this grid drawn out. Count every key — white and black — from one C to the next C and you pass exactly twelve keys. The numbers below each key are the semitone count from the left-hand C.

Notice the pattern of black keys

Black keys come in a group of two, then a group of three, then repeat. That grouping is your map: the white key just left of the group of two is always C. Find the two-black group and you can never be lost on a keyboard.

Why some notes have two names

A black key sits between two white keys, so it can be named from either side. The black key between C and D is C♯ ("C sharp", a semitone above C) or D♭ ("D flat", a semitone below D). Same key, two spellings — sharp means "one semitone up," flat means "one semitone down."1 Which spelling we use depends on the key we're in; you'll see that in Lesson 3.

Also notice: there is no black key between E–F and between B–C. Those white-key neighbours are already only a semitone apart.

The whole idea in one line

Twelve semitone steps, named C · C♯ · D · D♯ · E · F · F♯ · G · G♯ · A · A♯ · B, then it starts over at C an octave higher. Everything else is counting on this cycle.

Quick recall

Close-book retrieval beats re-reading. Answer from memory — getting one wrong is the point; the feedback is the lesson.
