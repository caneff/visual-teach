Building the Major Scale, Applied

One fixed recipe of whole and half steps turns any note into a key.

This is the lesson the whole course was aiming at — the mission itself: build the seven notes of any major key on purpose. You already have everything you need. From Lesson 1: the twelve-note grid. From Lesson 2: the whole step (2 semitones) and the half step (1). The major scale is just those two intervals in a fixed order.

The recipe

A major scale is seven notes, then the octave, spaced by this pattern of steps:1

W — W — H — W — W — W — H

whole · whole · half · whole · whole · whole · half  =  2·2·1·2·2·2·1 semitones

Start on any note, apply the seven steps, and you land on the seven scale notes plus the octave (which is why the last step brings you home). The pattern never changes — only the starting note does. That's the entire trick to constructing a key.

Why it sometimes needs a sharp or a flat

The recipe is counted in semitones, so it will sometimes land on a black key. When it does, we name that note so each letter A–G is used exactly once — which is why G major uses F♯ (not G♭) and F major uses B♭ (not A♯). Same key, the spelling that keeps the alphabet tidy.

Build it yourself

Pick a root, then click the seven notes in order, applying the recipe one step at a time. The keyboard checks every click against the pattern and tells you the next step. Start with C major (all white keys), then try G major and F major to watch a sharp and a flat appear — proof the pattern holds from any root.

Applied checklist — interleave everything

Now mix all three lessons together. This is interleaving on purpose: jumping between the keyboard, interval-counting, and the recipe is harder than drilling one at a time — and that difficulty is exactly what makes it stick. Work each item from memory, then expand to check.

1. Spell C major out loud (all seven notes + octave).

C – D – E – F – G – A – B – C. All white keys: the recipe's two half steps fall exactly on E–F and B–C, the pairs with no black key between.

2. From G, apply W-W-H-W-W-W-H. Where does step 6→7 (a whole step) land?

G – A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G. The whole step from E lands on F♯ (the black key), not F. G major has one sharp.

3. Build F major. Which note must be flattened, and why?

F – G – A – B♭ – C – D – E – F. Step 3→4 is a half step from A, landing on B♭. We call it B♭ (not A♯) so every letter A–G appears once.

4. What interval separates scale degree 1 and degree 5 in every major scale?

A perfect fifth — 7 semitones (2+2+1+2). Tonic to the fifth is the same distance in any key, which is why it always sounds equally "stable."

Your win — and the mission, reached

You can build any major scale from a single rule, including keys that need a sharp or a flat. You're no longer guessing which notes belong to a key — you're constructing them on purpose. That was the goal.

Spacing: come back tomorrow

Don't restack practice today. Memory is built by spacing — return after a day and rebuild C, G, and F major from a blank keyboard, plus one new root of your choice (try D major: it needs two sharps). If it feels a little effortful, it's working.
