Building the major scale, applied

One fixed pattern of whole and half steps turns any starting note into a major scale. Learn the recipe, then build keys on purpose — sharps and all.

⏱ ~15 min Needs: Lessons 1 & 2 complete Intermediate Lesson 3 of 3

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

  • Recall the major-scale step pattern: W–W–H–W–W–W–H.
  • Build the seven notes of a major scale from any starting note.
  • Explain why some keys need a sharp or a flat to keep the pattern.
Why this matters for your goal: this is the whole point — constructing the notes of a key on purpose. With one rule you'll stop guessing which notes "belong" and start building any major scale from scratch.

1The recipe: one pattern of steps

A major scale is seven notes picked out of the twelve by a fixed sequence of steps. Using the whole step (2 semitones) and half step (1 semitone) from Lesson 2, the pattern from your starting note (the root) is always:

W W H W W W H

Seven steps, starting on the root, land you back on the root an octave higher — and pick out the seven notes of the key along the way. Whole = skip a key; half = the very next key.

Why C major is "all white keys." Start on C and apply W–W–H–W–W–W–H: the half steps fall exactly where there's no black key (E→F and B→C). So you never need a black key — that's why C major is the plain white-key scale, and why every other key needs sharps or flats to keep the same pattern.

3Your applied checklist

Work these three in order with the builder above. Each adds one wrinkle — first the plain case, then a sharp, then a flat. Tick them off as you go; your progress is saved.

  • Build C major Root C. Confirm you land only on white keys, ending on C an octave up: C D E F G A B.
  • Build G major — needs one sharp Root G. The 7th step forces a black key: F♯, giving G A B C D E F♯. Without it, the final half step would break.
  • Build F major — needs one flat Root F. The 4th note is a black key named B♭, giving F G A B♭ C D E. Same pattern, a flat instead of a sharp.

4Make it stick

These mix all three lessons — recall each before revealing.

State the major-scale step pattern from memory.
Whole, whole, half, whole, whole, whole, half (W–W–H–W–W–W–H).
Spell the C major scale.
C, D, E, F, G, A, B — then back to C. All white keys.
Why does G major need an F♯ rather than an F?
The last two steps must be whole then half (E→F♯→G). A plain F would make E→F a half step and F→G a whole step — the wrong shape. Sharpening to F♯ restores W then H.
