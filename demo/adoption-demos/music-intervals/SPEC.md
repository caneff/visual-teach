# Shared spec — music theory: intervals and the major scale

Identical for both arms (control = teach-base only; treatment = teach-base +
visual-teach available). Pins the mission and a fixed 3-lesson plan. Each arm
authors freely _within_ this plan; wording may diverge, the topic series is
fixed. This spec must never mention the visual layer.

## Mission

Someone who enjoys music — maybe noodles on an instrument — has no theory. They
can't say why some notes sound right together, can't name the distance between
two notes, and couldn't build a scale if asked. The goal: understand the
twelve-note system, hear intervals as distances measured in semitones, and build
any major scale from a single rule — enough to stop guessing and actually
construct the notes of a key on purpose.

Auto-generated mission; the learner can edit it.

## Fixed lesson plan (exactly 3 lessons, in order)

1. **The twelve notes and the keyboard** — the smallest true mental model.
   Western music divides the octave into **twelve equal semitones** that repeat;
   a semitone is the smallest step. Use the piano keyboard as the map: the
   pattern of white and black keys, where the note names sit, and octave
   equivalence (the same note name recurring higher/lower). Build the
   "twelve repeating steps" intuition; no interval names or scales yet.

2. **Intervals: distance in semitones** — the core skill. An **interval** is the
   distance between two notes, counted in semitones; name the everyday ones
   (half step = 1, whole step = 2, major third = 4, perfect fifth = 7, octave =
   12) and the rough feel of consonance. Centered on a recall/decision feedback
   loop: given two notes on the keyboard, count and name the interval.

3. **Building the major scale, applied** — applied practice toward the mission.
   The major-scale recipe as a fixed pattern of steps
   (whole–whole–half–whole–whole–whole–half) applied from any starting note to
   pick out the seven notes of that key. Interleave lessons 1–2 into a short
   applied checklist: build C major, then a scale that needs a sharp or flat,
   confirming the pattern holds from any root.

## Constraints

- No music theory and cannot read staff notation. They CAN locate notes on a
  labeled keyboard diagram — use the keyboard as the primary model throughout;
  do not require reading the staff.
- Keep math to simple counting of semitones; no frequencies/physics.
- Make the keyboard and the note relationships visual and, where possible,
  something the learner can work through, not just read.
- One tangible win per lesson; keep each short.
