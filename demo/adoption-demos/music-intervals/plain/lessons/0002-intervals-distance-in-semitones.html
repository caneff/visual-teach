Intervals: distance in semitones

Stop saying “those two notes” — start naming the exact distance between them.

An interval is just the distance between two notes, and on the keyboard you measure it by counting semitones. Learn five everyday intervals and you can name most of what you hear — and start to explain why some pairs sound sweet and others tense.

Carrying over from Lesson 1: one semitone = the next key over (black keys counted); twelve semitones = one octave. Everything here is built on that ruler.

How to count an interval

Put a finger on the lower note, then count every key you cross — black and white — until you reach the upper note. The number of keys you move is the interval in semitones. Count the steps, not the note names. From C to G that is seven moves:

A first word on consonance

Some pairs sound settled and "open," others sound tense. As a rough rule, the octave (12) and the perfect fifth (7) are the most consonant — stable enough to end a song on. The major third (4) is sweet and bright. A bare half step (1) is the most dissonant — two keys rubbing right next to each other.2 You don't need the physics; you only need to connect the count to the feeling.

Your win this lesson Given any two keys, you can count the semitones between them and name the common intervals — 1 half, 2 whole, 4 major third, 7 fifth, 12 octave.
