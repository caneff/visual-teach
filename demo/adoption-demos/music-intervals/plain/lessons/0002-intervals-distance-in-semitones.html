Intervals — distance in semitones

Why do some notes sound “right” together? Because of the distance between them. Here's how to measure and name it.

From Lesson 1 you have the ruler: twelve semitones, repeating. An interval is just the distance between two notes, counted in semitones.1 That's the whole trick: put a finger on the lower note, walk up key by key — white and black — counting each step, and the number you land on names the interval.

How to count Count the gaps, not the keys. The lower note is “0.” Each key you move up — including black keys — adds one. Land on the higher note and read the total. That total is the interval's size.

The everyday intervals

You don't need all twelve at once. These five do most of the work, and you'll meet them constantly when we build scales next lesson:

1 · half step (semitone) 2 · whole step (tone) 4 · major third 7 · perfect fifth 12 · octave
IntervalSemitonesRough feel
Half step1tight, tense, “leaning”
Whole step2a clear, easy step
Major third4bright, sweet, “happy”
Perfect fifth7open, strong, stable
Octave12the same note, higher

That last column is consonance — roughly, how settled two notes sound together. Octaves and fifths feel rock-solid and open; the major third is the sweet, “happy” colour; a half step feels tense and wants to move.1 You don't need to memorise feelings — just notice them as you play.

Your win this lesson

Given any two notes on the keyboard, you can count the semitones between them and name the common interval. You're no longer guessing why notes clash or click — you can measure it.

Primary source — go deeper Open Music Theory — “Intervals.” The precise, trustworthy definition of interval size, with the same count-the-semitones logic. (Later it adds “quality” names — skim that part; counting is what you need now.) For drilling by ear, the musictheory.net trainers are excellent spaced practice between sessions.
