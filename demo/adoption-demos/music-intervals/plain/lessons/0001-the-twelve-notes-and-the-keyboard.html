The twelve notes and the keyboard

Before you can measure the distance between two notes, you need the ruler. Here it is — twelve little steps that repeat forever.

You already enjoy making sounds on your instrument. The thing that turns guessing into knowing is a single, small mental model: Western music takes the span from one note to the same note higher — an octave — and cuts it into twelve equal steps. Each step is called a semitone, and a semitone is the smallest move you can make.1

The piano keyboard is simply a picture of those twelve steps. We'll use it as our map for the whole course — no staff notation, ever. Every key, white or black, is one semitone from its neighbour.

Read the map

Notice the black keys come in tidy groups of two and three. That pattern is your anchor: the white key just to the left of the pair of black keys is always C. Find C and you can name everything else.

The seven letters A B C D E F G name the white keys; the black keys borrow a neighbour's name with a sharp (♯) or flat (♭). More on naming black keys in Lesson 2.

Count the twelve steps

Press the button to number one octave's worth of keys. Step 0 is a C; by the time you reach a C again you've taken twelve semitones — and the cycle starts over.

Two octaves shown. The same twelve names repeat in each.
The big idea Same letter name, higher or lower box on the keyboard = the same note, just in another octave. This is octave equivalence: a C is a C is a C.2 That's why there are really only twelve note names, not eighty-eight.

Your win this lesson
