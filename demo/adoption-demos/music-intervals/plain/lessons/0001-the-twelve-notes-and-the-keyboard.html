The twelve notes and the keyboard

The smallest true map of all of music — and it fits in one octave.

Western music is built from just twelve evenly spaced notes. Past the twelfth, the names start over. Learn that one repeating ruler and the whole keyboard stops being a wall of keys and becomes a map you can read.

One step at a time: the semitone

The smallest move in Western music is the semitone — also called a half step. On a piano it is the move from any key to the key immediately next to it, counting the black keys.1 C up to the black key C♯ is one semitone. E up to F — where there is no black key between them — is also one semitone.

Stack twelve of these steps and something remarkable happens: you arrive at a note that sounds like where you began, only higher. That distance is the octave, and those twelve steps are the entire alphabet of pitch.2

The keyboard is the map

The seven white keys carry the letter names A B C D E F G; after G the letters wrap back to A. The five black keys are the in-between notes (the sharps and flats). White and black together make the twelve. Notice the black keys come in a group of two then a group of three — that visual rhythm is how you find your bearings without reading a single number.

One octave, C to C. Find the two-black-key group: the white key just to its left is always C. That is your anchor anywhere on a real piano.

Count the twelve, then watch them repeat

Here is the full chromatic climb from C — every semitone, numbered. Step 12 is another C: same name, same character, just an octave higher. That sameness is called octave equivalence, and it is why a short twelve-note pattern is enough to describe a piano with dozens of keys — the pattern simply repeats.3

C·0 C♯·1 D·2 D♯·3 E·4 F·5 F♯·6 G·7 G♯·8 A·9 A♯·10 B·11 C·12

Twelve equal semitones span the octave; note 0 and note 12 share the name C. The black keys also carry flat names (C♯ = D♭, etc.) — more on that in Lesson 2.

Your win this lesson You can look at any keyboard, find C from the two-black-key group, and explain why the keys keep repeating: twelve semitones, then the names start over.
