Building the major scale

One rule, applied from any note, gives you the seven notes of a key. This is the payoff — building notes on purpose.

Here's where Lessons 1 and 2 cash in. A major scale is just seven notes picked out of the twelve by a fixed recipe of steps — and the steps are intervals you already know: whole step = 2 semitones, half step = 1.

The one rule

Start on any note. Then walk up by this exact pattern of whole (W) and half (H) steps, and you'll land on the seven notes of that major scale before arriving back at the octave:1

WWH WWW H

W–W–H–W–W–W–H. The two half steps fall between scale steps 3→4 and 7→8. That's the entire secret; every major scale obeys it.2

Pick a starting note. The builder applies W–W–H–W–W–W–H for you, lights the seven notes plus the octave, and numbers the scale degrees. Watch what happens when the pattern forces you onto a black key.

Why the sharps and flats appear C major is the freebie: the pattern lands only on white keys — C D E F G A B. Start anywhere else and the same pattern eventually demands a black key. We give it the neighbouring letter's name with a ♯ or ♭ so that every scale uses each letter A–G exactly once. That's why G major needs F♯ (not G♭) and F major needs B♭ (not A♯).2

The build checklist

Three steps you can do anywhere, on any instrument:

  1. Pick the root and call it scale step 1.
  2. Apply W–W–H–W–W–W–H, counting semitones (2,2,1,2,2,2,1).
  3. Name each note with the next letter (root letter, then the next, and so on) — adding a ♯ or ♭ only as the count requires.

Your turn — interleaved practice

This mixes everything: the step pattern, the interval counting from Lesson 2, and real scale spellings. Mixing topics (interleaving) is harder than drilling one — and that difficulty is exactly what builds lasting memory. Use the builder above if you get stuck.

Your win this lesson — and the mission

You can now build any major scale from any note using one rule, and you understand why a key needs the sharps or flats it does. That was the whole goal: stop guessing, and construct the notes of a key on purpose. From here, every other scale, chord, and key signature is a variation on what you just learned.
