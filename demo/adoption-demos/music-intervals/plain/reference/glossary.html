Glossary

The course's working vocabulary. These definitions are fixed — every lesson uses the words this way. Built for quick lookup and clean printing.

Semitone (half step)
The smallest distance in this system — one key to the very next key on the piano, white or black. Counts as 1. The ruler everything else is measured with.
Whole step (tone)
Two semitones, 2. Skips one key in between.
Octave
The distance from a note up to the next note of the same name — 12 semitones. The span the twelve notes divide.
Octave equivalence
Notes with the same letter name, any number of octaves apart, are treated as the “same” note. A C is a C is a C. Why there are only twelve note names.
Pitch class
One of the twelve named notes considered apart from its octave (C, C♯/D♭, D, … B). The keyboard repeats these twelve forever.
Interval
The distance between two notes, counted in semitones. Count the gaps from the lower note (0) up to the higher one.
Consonance / dissonance
Roughly, how settled (consonant) or tense (dissonant) two notes sound together. Octaves and perfect fifths are very consonant; a half step is tense. A feel, not a formula.
Major third
An interval of 4 semitones. Bright, sweet, “happy.”
Perfect fifth
An interval of 7 semitones. Open and stable — the strongest consonance after the octave.
Major scale
Seven notes selected from the twelve by the fixed step pattern W–W–H–W–W–W–H (semitones 2‑2‑1‑2‑2‑2‑1), then the octave.
Root (tonic)
The note a scale starts on and is named after. Scale step 1.
Scale degree
A note's numbered position in the scale, 1 through 7 (then 8 = the octave of 1). Step 5, for example, is a perfect fifth above the root.
Sharp (♯) / Flat (♭)
A sharp raises a note by one semitone; a flat lowers it by one. Used to name the black key the scale pattern lands on, so each letter A–G is used once.
Accidental
Any sharp or flat applied to a note. C major has none; every other major scale needs at least one.
Anchor that ties it all together Find C (the white key just left of the two black keys). From there, count semitones to measure any interval, and apply W–W–H–W–W–W–H to build any major scale.
