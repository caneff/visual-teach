Reference / Twelve Notes

Glossary

The shared vocabulary of this course. Every lesson uses these terms exactly as defined here.

This is the canonical word-list — when a lesson says semitone or whole step, it means precisely what is written below. Keep it open beside the lessons.

Octave
The distance from one note to the next note of the same name, higher or lower (e.g. one C up to the next C). It spans 12 semitones.
Octave equivalence
The fact that notes one or more octaves apart share a name and sound like "the same note, higher/lower." This is why the keyboard is a short pattern that simply repeats.
Semitone (half step, half tone)
The smallest step in Western music: the move from any key to the very next key, counting black keys. C→C♯ and E→F are each one semitone. Def: Wikipedia, “Semitone.”
Whole tone (whole step)
Two semitones. C→D is a whole step (it skips over C♯).
Chromatic scale
All twelve semitones of the octave played in order — the complete set of pitches before any are chosen out.
Natural notes
The seven lettered notes A B C D E F G — the white keys. After G the letters wrap back to A.
Sharp (♯) / Flat (♭)
A sharp raises a natural note by one semitone; a flat lowers it by one. Most black keys have two names — C♯ and D♭ are the same key (enharmonic).
Interval
The distance between two notes, measured in this course as a count of semitones.
Half step / Whole step (as intervals)
1 semitone / 2 semitones — the two smallest intervals.
Major third
An interval of 4 semitones (e.g. C→E). Bright, "happy" colour; the bottom of a major chord.
Perfect fifth
An interval of 7 semitones (e.g. C→G). Very stable and consonant — the most "open" sound short of the octave.
Consonance / Dissonance
Roughly, how settled (consonant) or tense (dissonant) two notes sound together. Octaves and perfect fifths are strongly consonant; a single semitone is sharply dissonant.
Root (tonic)
The note a scale or key is built from and named after — the "home" note. C major has the root C.
Major scale
The seven notes you get by applying the step pattern W–W–H–W–W–W–H from a root, then arriving back at the root an octave up.
Step pattern
The fixed sequence of whole (W) and half (H) steps that defines a scale. For the major scale it is always W–W–H–W–W–W–H, in every key. Hutchinson, Open Music Theory.
Tetrachord
A four-note group. A major scale is two tetrachords, each shaped W–W–H, joined by a whole step in the middle.
