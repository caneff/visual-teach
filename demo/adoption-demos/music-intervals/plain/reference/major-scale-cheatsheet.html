Major-scale cheat sheet

One pattern, every key. Print this and keep it on the music stand.

The one rule

Start on any note (the root). Walk up the keyboard following this fixed sequence of whole (W = 2 semitones) and half (H = 1 semitone) steps. After the seventh step you land back on the root, an octave up.

WWH WWW H

W–W–H–W–W–W–H. Two halves of the same shape: W–W–H · (whole step) · W–W–H — two tetrachords. Source: Hutchinson, Open Music Theory.

C major on the map

From C, the pattern lands entirely on white keys — which is exactly why C major has no sharps or flats.

How to build one in your head

  1. Write the seven letter names starting from your root, in order, using each letter once (e.g. for D: D E F G A B C).
  2. Walk the pattern W W H W W W H, counting semitones on the keyboard from each note to the next.
  3. Wherever the count comes up short or long, sharpen or flatten that letter to fix the step. Never change a letter's name — only add ♯ or ♭.
  4. Check: you should arrive back on the root after the final H.
Semitone quick-reference: half step = 1 · whole step = 2 · major third = 4 · perfect fifth = 7 · octave = 12. See the glossary.
