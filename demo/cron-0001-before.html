Cron & scheduling · Lesson 1

Reading a cron expression

Five fields, left to right, decide exactly when a job runs — learn to read any line at a glance.

⏱ ~12 min · Needs: terminal access · Nothing to install

1The five fields

A cron expression is five numbers separated by spaces. Position is everything: the scheduler reads them left to right.

*   *   *   *   * minute  ·  hour  ·  day of month  ·  month  ·  day of week

FieldRangeNotes
minute0–59
hour0–2324-hour clock
day of month1–31
month1–12
day of week0–60 (and 7) is Sunday

A bare * means "every value of this field," so * * * * * means every minute of every hour of every day. The whole skill is narrowing those stars down.

Key idea: cron checks the clock once a minute and runs every job whose five fields all match the current time. There is no field smaller than a minute.

2The four operators

Four small operators turn a star into a real schedule. Each one acts inside a single field.

OperatorMeansExample in the minute field
*every value* → every minute
*/nevery n steps*/15 → minute 0, 15, 30, 45
,a list0,30 → minute 0 and 30
-a range0-4 → minutes 0 through 4

They combine. 0,30 9-17 * * * runs on the hour and half-hour, but only between 9am and 5pm. Read it field by field and it falls open.

Watch the slash. */15 in the hour field is every 15 hours, not every 15 minutes. The operator is the same; the field it sits in changes the unit.

3A worked example

Here is a nightly backup. Open your user crontab with crontab -e and add a line:

# ┌ minute  ┌ hour  ┌ day  ┌ month  ┌ weekday
# │         │       │      │        │
30 2 * * *  /usr/local/bin/backup.sh   # 02:30 every day
0 7 * * 1-5 /usr/local/bin/report.sh   # 07:00 Mon–Fri

The editor crontab -e opens is usually vi: press Esc, type :wq, and hit Enter to save. List your jobs anytime with crontab -l.

Timezone trap: system cron runs in the server's timezone, not yours. A job set for 30 2 * * * fires at 2:30am server time — on a UTC server that may be the middle of your afternoon. Check timedatectl before you trust a schedule.

4Two ways to say "every 15 minutes"

Beginners often reach for a comma list when a step would do. Both run at the same times, but one scales and one does not.

Need0,15,30,45 * * * **/15 * * * *
Reads clearlyyesyes
Easy to change the intervalno — rewrite the listyes — edit one number
Stays short for fine intervalsnoyes
Reach for */n. The comma list works, but you hand-maintain it; change the interval and you rewrite the whole thing. The step form changes with one number.

5Hands-on: add a job

Add the nightly backup yourself. Check off steps as you go — progress is saved in this page.

  1. Run crontab -e in a terminal.
  2. Type 30 2 * * * /usr/local/bin/backup.sh on its own line.
  3. Esc, then :wq, then Enter.
  4. Run crontab -l and check your line is listed.

6Check yourself

1. What does */15 9-17 * * 1-5 run?

2. Which expression runs at exactly 6:00am every day?

🎓 Stuck or curious? Your teacher is the agent that made this. Ask it anything — "why is there no 'every second' field?", "how do I test a schedule without waiting?", "what does 0 0 1 * * do?". Follow-up questions are the point, not an interruption.

7What you just earned

You can read any standard five-field cron line, reach for */n over hand-written lists, and you know to check the server timezone before trusting a schedule. Next lesson: the special strings (@daily, @reboot) and how environment differs under cron.
